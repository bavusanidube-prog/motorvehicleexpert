MOT corrosion guide

Can Rust Fail MOT?

Yes, rust can fail an MOT in the UK if corrosion weakens structural areas, affects safety-related mounting points, exposes brake or suspension components to risk, or creates dangerous sharp edges. Light surface rust is different from serious corrosion.

Quick answer

Rust becomes an MOT issue when corrosion weakens the vehicle structure or affects safety-related areas such as suspension mounts, seatbelt anchor points, steering components, brake pipes, subframes, sills or chassis sections.

Light surface rust on non-structural panels may not fail, but bubbling, holes, flaking metal, sharp edges or corrosion close to important mounting points should be checked before the test.

When rust can fail an MOT

MOT testers are especially concerned with corrosion that affects structural strength, prescribed areas, safety-related components or the overall condition of the vehicle. Rust is not judged only by how ugly it looks. Its location and severity matter more.

Structural corrosion

Rust that weakens chassis, sills, subframes or load-bearing areas can fail.

Rust near mountings

Corrosion near suspension, steering, brakes or seatbelt anchorages is taken seriously.

Holes or perforation

Metal eaten through by rust can fail, especially in structural or prescribed areas.

Sharp edges

Corroded bodywork with dangerous sharp edges can fail if it could injure someone.

Poor rust repairs

Weak welding, poor plating or unsafe repairs can still fail the MOT.

Brake pipe corrosion

Rusty or weakened brake pipes are a serious safety concern.

Surface rust vs serious rust

Surface rust

Light staining or top-layer rust is often cosmetic if the metal remains strong and is not in a critical area. It may still be worth treating early before it spreads.

Serious corrosion

Metal that flakes away, feels weak, has holes, breaks through when pressed or affects mountings is much more likely to fail.

Early treatment

Treating minor rust early is usually cheaper than waiting until welding, replacement panels or structural repairs are needed.

Hidden rust

Inner sills, floor edges, subframes and suspension mountings can rust before outer panels look bad.

Why prescribed areas matter

In MOT terms, rust near important mounting points is more serious because it can affect the strength of parts that support braking, steering, suspension, seatbelts or the vehicle structure. A small rusty hole in the wrong place can matter more than a larger cosmetic patch on a non-structural panel.

  • ✓Rust near suspension mounts can affect how securely the suspension is supported.
  • ✓Rust near seatbelt anchorages can affect occupant safety.
  • ✓Rust near steering or braking components can become safety-critical.
  • ✓Rust in load-bearing areas can reduce the strength of the vehicle structure.
  • ✓Rust repairs must be strong and properly carried out, not just covered over.

Common rust MOT fail areas

  • 1Sills and jacking points: especially inner sills, outer sills and areas close to structural seams.
  • 2Subframes and chassis rails: rust here can affect structural strength and component mounting.
  • 3Suspension mounting points: corrosion near arms, springs, shocks or subframe mounts is important.
  • 4Brake pipes and brake pipe fixings: heavy corrosion can become a braking safety issue.
  • 5Seatbelt anchorage points: rust near these areas can be a serious MOT concern.
  • 6Floor pans near structural sections: holes or weak areas can fail if they affect strength.
  • 7Wheel arches: can fail if sharp, badly corroded or structurally affected.
  • 8Body panels: can fail if corrosion creates unsafe sharp edges.

What to check before your MOT

1. Inspect the sills

Look along outer and inner sills for bubbling, holes, flaky metal, fresh filler, soft areas or poor previous repairs.

2. Check underneath

Use a torch to inspect floor edges, subframes, chassis rails, suspension mounts and jacking points.

3. Look at brake pipes

Heavy rust on metal brake lines should be checked before the test, especially around bends and fixings.

4. Inspect wheel arches

Check for sharp edges, loose rusty metal, spreading corrosion or rust close to structural sections.

5. Read old MOT advisories

Previous corrosion advisories often predict future failures if left untreated.

6. Repair early

Minor corrosion repairs are usually easier before rust spreads into structural areas.

Can you drive with rust?

Minor cosmetic rust may not affect safety immediately. Structural corrosion is different because it can weaken the car and affect crash protection, suspension strength, steering stability or braking safety.

If rust is severe, holed, sharp, close to mounting points or repeatedly mentioned on MOT advisories, get the vehicle inspected properly.

Typical rust repair options

Surface treatment

Usually lower cost if caught early before metal becomes weak. This may involve cleaning, treating, priming and protecting the area.

Local welding

Moderate cost depending on access, replacement metal, labour time and paint finish.

Structural repair

Can become expensive if sills, floors, mounts, subframes or chassis sections are badly affected.

Avoid simply covering rust with filler, thick paint or underseal. It may hide the problem briefly, but it will not restore weakened structure.

Rust advisories: should you worry?

A corrosion advisory means the tester has noticed rust that is not bad enough to fail at the time of the test. It should still be taken seriously because rust usually gets worse if ignored.

  • ✓One minor advisory may be manageable if treated early.
  • !Repeated corrosion advisories suggest delayed repair or worsening rust.
  • !Advisories mentioning prescribed areas, sills, subframes or brake pipes deserve extra caution.
  • !Rust advisories on older cars should affect repair planning and used car value.

Rust and used car buying

Rust matters a lot when buying a used car, especially older vehicles. A car can look tidy on top but have corrosion underneath, around sills, subframes or suspension mounting points.

  • ✓Check MOT history for repeated corrosion advisories.
  • ✓Inspect sills, wheel arches, jacking points and floor edges.
  • ✓Be cautious of fresh underseal hiding poor metal.
  • ✓Budget carefully if welding is likely.
  • ✓Walk away if structural rust is severe and the seller dismisses it as cosmetic.

Frequently asked questions

Can surface rust fail an MOT?

Usually not on its own if it is cosmetic and not affecting structure, safety or creating sharp edges.

Can rusty sills fail an MOT?

Yes. Rusty sills can fail if they are holed, weakened, badly repaired or close to structural or safety-related areas.

Can rusted brake pipes fail?

Yes. Heavily corroded brake pipes are a common MOT issue because they can affect braking safety.

Will rust get an advisory first?

Sometimes. Minor corrosion may receive an advisory, but serious corrosion can fail immediately.

Is underseal enough to pass an MOT?

No. Underseal may protect good metal, but it does not properly repair weakened or holed structure.

Can poor welding fail an MOT?

Yes. Rust repairs must be strong and properly carried out. Poor plating, weak welding or unsafe repairs can still fail.

What is the best action before an MOT?

Inspect common rust spots early, repair advisories before they worsen and avoid simply covering rust.
