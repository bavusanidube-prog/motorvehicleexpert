Can Rust Fail MOT?

Yes, rust can fail an MOT in the UK if corrosion weakens structural areas, affects safety-related mounting points, exposes brake or suspension components to risk, or creates dangerous sharp edges. Light surface rust is different from serious corrosion.

Quick answer

Rust becomes an MOT issue when corrosion weakens the vehicle structure or affects safety-related areas such as suspension mounts, seatbelt anchor points, steering components, brake pipes, subframes, sills or chassis sections.

Light surface rust on non-structural panels may not fail, but bubbling, holes, flaking metal, sharp edges or corrosion close to important mounting points should be checked before the test.

When rust can fail an MOT

Structural corrosion

Rust that weakens chassis, sills, subframes or load-bearing areas can fail.

Rust near mountings

Corrosion near suspension, steering, brakes or seatbelt anchorages is taken seriously.

Holes or perforation

Metal eaten through by rust can fail, especially in structural or prescribed areas.

Sharp edges

Corroded bodywork with dangerous sharp edges can fail if it could injure someone.

Poor rust repairs

Weak welding, poor plating or unsafe repairs can still fail the MOT.

Brake pipe corrosion

Rusty or weakened brake pipes are a serious safety concern.

Surface rust vs serious rust

Surface rust

Light staining or top-layer rust is often cosmetic if the metal remains strong and is not in a critical area.

Serious corrosion

Metal that flakes away, feels weak, has holes or affects mountings is much more likely to fail.

Early treatment

Treating minor rust early is usually cheaper than waiting until welding is needed.

Hidden rust

Inner sills, floor edges, subframes and suspension mountings can rust before outer panels look bad.

Common rust MOT fail areas

  • 1Sills and jacking points.
  • 2Subframes and chassis rails.
  • 3Suspension mounting points.
  • 4Brake pipes and brake pipe fixings.
  • 5Seatbelt anchorage points.
  • 6Floor pans near structural sections.
  • 7Wheel arches if sharp, badly corroded or structurally affected.
  • 8Body panels if corrosion creates unsafe sharp edges.

What to check before your MOT

1. Inspect the sills

Look along outer and inner sills for bubbling, holes, flaky metal or soft areas.

2. Check underneath

Use a torch to inspect floor edges, subframes, chassis rails and suspension mounts.

3. Look at brake pipes

Heavy rust on metal brake lines should be checked before the test.

4. Inspect wheel arches

Check for sharp edges, loose rusty metal or spreading corrosion.

5. Read old MOT advisories

Previous corrosion advisories often predict future failures if left untreated.

6. Repair early

Minor corrosion repairs are usually easier before rust spreads into structural areas.

Can you drive with rust?

Minor cosmetic rust may not affect safety immediately. Structural corrosion is different because it can weaken the car and affect crash protection, suspension strength, steering stability or braking safety.

If rust is severe, holed, sharp, close to mounting points or repeatedly mentioned on MOT advisories, get the vehicle inspected properly.

Typical rust repair options

Surface treatment

Usually lower cost if caught early before metal becomes weak.

Local welding

Moderate cost depending on access, replacement metal and paint finish.

Structural repair

Can become expensive if sills, floors, mounts or subframes are badly affected.

Rust and used car buying

Rust matters a lot when buying a used car, especially older vehicles. A car can look tidy on top but have corrosion underneath, around sills, subframes or suspension mounting points.

  • ✓Check MOT history for repeated corrosion advisories.
  • ✓Inspect sills, wheel arches, jacking points and floor edges.
  • ✓Be cautious of fresh underseal hiding poor metal.
  • ✓Budget carefully if welding is likely.

Frequently asked questions

Can surface rust fail an MOT?

Usually not on its own if it is cosmetic and not affecting structure, safety or creating sharp edges.

Can rusty sills fail an MOT?

Yes. Rusty sills can fail if they are holed, weakened or close to structural or safety-related areas.

Can rusted brake pipes fail?

Yes. Heavily corroded brake pipes are a common MOT issue because they can affect braking safety.

Will rust get an advisory first?

Sometimes. Minor corrosion may receive an advisory, but serious corrosion can fail immediately.

Is underseal enough to pass an MOT?

No. Underseal may protect good metal, but it does not properly repair weakened or holed structure.

What is the best action before an MOT?

Inspect common rust spots early, repair advisories before they worsen and avoid simply covering rust.
