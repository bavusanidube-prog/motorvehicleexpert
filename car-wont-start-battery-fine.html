Starting problems guide

Car won’t start but battery seems fine

If your car will not start but the lights, radio and dashboard still work, the battery may not be the only possible cause. The fault could involve the starter motor, ignition switch, immobiliser, fuel system, relays, sensors, wiring or battery connections.

Quick answer

A car can seem to have a good battery because the lights and dashboard work, but starting the engine needs much more power. If the engine does not crank, the issue may be starter, battery connection, relay, immobiliser or ignition related.

If the engine cranks normally but does not fire, the fault may be fuel, ignition, sensor, immobiliser or engine management related.

First question: does the engine crank?

No crank

If you turn the key or press start and the engine does not turn over, suspect battery voltage, terminals, starter motor, starter relay, ignition switch, clutch switch, park/neutral switch or immobiliser issues.

Cranks but does not start

If the engine turns over normally but will not fire, suspect fuel delivery, ignition spark, engine sensors, immobiliser, timing or engine management faults.

Common causes when the battery seems fine

  • 1Weak battery under load: lights may work, but the battery may still fail when the starter needs high current.
  • 2Loose or corroded terminals: poor connections can stop enough power reaching the starter motor.
  • 3Bad earth strap: a poor ground connection can cause clicking, no crank or electrical faults.
  • 4Starter motor fault: a worn starter can click, stick or fail to turn the engine.
  • 5Starter relay or ignition switch: the start signal may not reach the starter correctly.
  • 6Immobiliser or key issue: the car may block starting if the key is not recognised.
  • 7Fuel pump or fuel pressure fault: the engine may crank but not receive enough fuel.
  • 8Crankshaft or camshaft sensor fault: the engine ECU may not know when to inject fuel or trigger spark.

What to check first

1. Listen carefully

A single click, rapid clicking or silence can each point to different starting faults.

2. Watch the dashboard

If lights dim heavily or go out when starting, suspect low voltage or poor battery connections.

3. Check gear position

Make sure the car is in Park or Neutral, or the clutch is fully pressed on manual cars.

4. Inspect terminals

Look for loose clamps, corrosion, damaged cables or poor earth connections.

5. Try the spare key

If the immobiliser or key recognition is suspected, a spare key may help confirm the issue.

6. Avoid repeated attempts

Constant starting attempts can flatten the battery and make diagnosis harder.

If it clicks

Clicking often points to low battery voltage, poor terminals, bad earth, starter solenoid or starter motor failure.

If it cranks normally

A normal crank with no start often points towards fuel, spark, sensor, immobiliser or engine management faults.

If nothing happens

No sound at all can involve ignition switch, starter relay, immobiliser, clutch switch, park switch or wiring faults.

When to stop guessing

If the car repeatedly fails to start, do not keep replacing parts at random. A proper diagnostic process should check battery condition under load, starter current draw, charging output, fault codes, immobiliser status, fuel pressure and ignition signals.

Guessing can quickly become expensive. A car that “seems like the battery is fine” can still have a weak battery, bad connection or charging fault.

Frequently asked questions

Can a battery be bad even if the lights work?

Yes. Lights and electronics need much less power than the starter motor.

Could it still be the starter motor?

Yes. A bad starter motor is common when the engine does not crank properly.

Why does my car crank but not start?

Common causes include no fuel pressure, no spark, immobiliser issues, crank sensor faults or engine management problems.

Can an immobiliser stop the car starting?

Yes. If the key is not recognised, many vehicles will prevent starting.

Should I keep trying to start it?

No. Repeated attempts can flatten the battery and make the original fault harder to diagnose.
