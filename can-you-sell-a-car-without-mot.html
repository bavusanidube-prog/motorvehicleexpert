Can You Sell a Car Without MOT?

Yes, you can usually sell a car without a valid MOT in the UK, but you must describe it honestly. A car with no MOT is harder to sell, usually worth less, and the buyer cannot simply drive it away for normal road use.

Quick answer

You can sell a car without MOT, but make it clear in the advert and sale messages that the vehicle has no current MOT. Do not imply it is road-ready if it is not.

Expect lower offers because the buyer is taking on repair risk, MOT risk and transport arrangements.

Is it legal to sell a car without MOT?

Yes, a private owner can usually sell a car without MOT. The important part is honesty: the buyer should know the MOT status, known faults and whether the car is safe or not.

Private sale

Usually allowed if the car is described accurately.

Trade sale

Dealers, traders and scrap buyers may still buy no-MOT vehicles.

No false claims

Do not describe it as MOT’d, roadworthy or ready to drive if it is not.

MOT history visible

Buyers can check MOT status, failures, advisories and mileage online.

Fault disclosure

Mention known safety, warning light, rust, tyre or brake issues.

Receipt helps

Write a clear sale receipt showing the no-MOT status.

How can the buyer collect a car with no MOT?

A car without MOT should not be used for normal driving on public roads. The safest collection method is a trailer, recovery truck or transport service.

There is a limited situation where a vehicle may be driven to a pre-booked MOT appointment, but it still needs to be insured and roadworthy. The journey should be direct and genuinely for the test.

  • 1Best option: trailer or recovery vehicle.
  • 2Possible limited option: direct journey to a pre-booked MOT.
  • 3Insurance: the buyer must have valid cover if driving.
  • 4Roadworthiness: unsafe cars should not be driven.

How selling without MOT affects value

A missing MOT usually reduces value because the buyer cannot be sure what repairs are needed. Even if the car seems fine, the buyer may budget for tyres, brakes, suspension, rust, emissions or warning light repairs.

Lower offers

Most buyers discount the price to cover repair and transport risk.

Smaller buyer pool

Many private buyers avoid cars they cannot legally drive away.

More trade interest

Traders and mechanics may buy if the price reflects the risk.

Repair uncertainty

Failed emissions, rust or brake faults can become expensive.

Transport costs

Recovery or trailer collection may reduce what the buyer offers.

Good history helps

Receipts and honest detail can improve confidence.

Should you get an MOT before selling?

Often, yes. If the car is likely to pass or only needs small repairs, a fresh MOT can make it easier to sell and may increase the price by more than the test cost.

Worth considering

If the car is roadworthy, has no major warning lights and previous MOT history is reasonable.

Maybe not worth it

If the car has obvious major faults, severe rust, engine problems or high repair risk.

Fresh MOT benefit

Buyers can drive away more easily if tax and insurance are sorted.

Failed MOT risk

A failed MOT creates a public record of defects and may reduce value further.

Paperwork when selling without MOT

  • 1Give the buyer the green new keeper slip from the V5C.
  • 2Tell DVLA you have sold the vehicle.
  • 3Write a receipt with date, time, mileage, price and buyer details.
  • 4State clearly: “Sold without current MOT”.
  • 5List known faults or attach a copy of the advert description.
  • 6Keep copies of messages and payment evidence.

GOV.UK says sellers should give the green new keeper slip to the buyer and tell DVLA when the vehicle is sold. Buyers can also check MOT history online, including pass/fail records, mileage and advisories.

What buyers usually check

Buyers are likely to check the MOT history, mileage pattern, service evidence and the reason there is no current MOT. They may also inspect obvious failure areas.

MOT history

Past failures, advisories, mileage gaps and repeated defects.

Rust

Sills, subframes, jacking points, wheel arches and brake pipes.

Tyres and brakes

Low tread, bulges, grinding brakes or warning lights.

Warning lights

Engine, ABS, airbag, brake, TPMS or DPF warnings.

Engine and clutch

Smoke, overheating, slipping clutch, misfire or power loss.

Paper trail

Invoices, service book, repair receipts and ownership history.

How to write the advert

Be clear and direct. A good advert can still attract serious buyers if it explains the MOT status honestly.

  • ✓State the MOT expiry date or say “no current MOT”.
  • ✓Mention known faults and whether the car starts and drives on private land.
  • ✓Include mileage, service history and recent repairs.
  • ✓Explain collection requirements clearly.
  • ✓Avoid phrases like “will pass MOT easily” unless you genuinely know.

Frequently asked questions

Can I legally sell a car with no MOT?

Yes, usually, as long as you describe the car honestly and do not mislead the buyer.

Can the buyer drive it away?

Not for normal road use. They may need transport, recovery or a genuine pre-booked MOT journey with insurance.

Will no MOT reduce the price?

Usually yes. Buyers often deduct for unknown repairs, MOT risk and collection costs.

Should I MOT it before selling?

Often yes if it is likely to pass. A fresh MOT can make the car easier to sell.

Can I sell a failed MOT car?

Yes, but disclose the failure and known defects clearly.

What should I put on the receipt?

Include date, time, mileage, price, buyer details and a note that it is sold without current MOT.
