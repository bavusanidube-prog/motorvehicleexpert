Quick answer

A warning light should be judged by its colour, whether it is steady or flashing, and how the car feels. Red lights, flashing lights, brake warnings, oil pressure warnings, overheating, charging faults and steering warnings usually need faster attention.

Amber lights often mean the car has detected a fault but may still drive. That does not mean they should be ignored. If the car loses power, overheats, smokes, shakes, struggles to brake or becomes hard to steer, stop safely and arrange inspection.

What warning light colour usually means

Red warning light

Usually more urgent. Red lights often relate to safety, braking, oil pressure, overheating, charging or systems that may make the car unsafe.

Amber or yellow warning light

Usually means a fault needs attention soon. The car may still drive, but the system has detected a problem.

Green or blue light

Often means a system is active, such as lights or main beam, rather than a fault.

A flashing warning light is usually more urgent than a steady one. For example, a flashing engine management light can point to a misfire or emissions damage risk.

Warning lights you should not ignore

Some lights can point to faults that affect safety or engine damage risk. These should be treated more seriously, especially if the car also feels different.

What to do when a warning light appears

1. Check the colour

Red warnings usually need faster action than amber warnings.

2. Notice symptoms

Power loss, overheating, smoke, brake changes or steering changes make the issue more urgent.

3. Use the app

The diagnostic app can help you check possible causes, safe-to-drive guidance and MOT implications.

Open diagnostic app →

4. Check the handbook

Symbols vary by manufacturer, so confirm the meaning for your vehicle.

5. Read fault codes

Fault-code reading helps identify the affected system, but codes still need interpretation.

6. Fix the cause

Clearing the warning without repairing the fault usually means it will return.

Common warning light mistakes

  • !Ignoring a red light because the car still drives.
  • !Clearing fault codes without fixing the cause.
  • !Assuming a battery light means only the battery is faulty.
  • !Driving with an oil pressure warning.
  • !Leaving warning lights until MOT day.
  • !Buying a used car with warning lights on without diagnosis.

Frequently asked questions

Can I keep driving with a warning light on?

It depends on the light and symptoms. Red, flashing, brake, oil pressure, overheating, charging or steering warnings need more urgent attention.

Does a warning light always mean an expensive repair?

No. Some faults are simple, but diagnosis is needed before guessing at parts.

Can a warning light fail an MOT?

Yes. Some lights can affect MOT results, especially airbag, ABS, engine/emissions, TPMS and safety-system warnings.

What does an amber warning light mean?

An amber warning usually means a fault needs attention soon. The car may still drive, but it should not be ignored.

What does a red warning light mean?

A red warning usually means stop safely or act urgently, especially if it relates to brakes, oil pressure, overheating, steering or charging.

Is a flashing warning light serious?

Usually yes. A flashing warning light is normally more urgent than a steady one and should be checked quickly.
