Car Overheats in Traffic

If your car runs normally on open roads but overheats in slow traffic, queues or while stationary, the fault often involves low-speed cooling. Common causes include a failed radiator fan, low coolant, radiator restriction, thermostat faults, airlocks or poor coolant circulation.

Quick answer

A car that overheats in traffic but cools down when moving is often struggling because there is not enough airflow through the radiator at low speed. The cooling fan, fan relay, fan fuse, radiator, coolant level and thermostat are common areas to check.

If the temperature gauge keeps rising, steam appears, the heater blows cold while the engine is hot, or a warning light comes on, stop safely and let the engine cool before checking anything.

Why cooling fan faults show in traffic

When the car is moving, air naturally passes through the radiator. In traffic, that airflow drops, so the electric cooling fan must pull air through instead. If the fan, relay, fuse, wiring, temperature sensor or fan control module fails, the engine can get hot while stationary.

Fan never runs

Could be a fan motor, fuse, relay, wiring, sensor or fan control issue.

Fan runs constantly

May point to overheating, sensor faults, air conditioning demand or control issues.

Temperature drops when moving

Often supports an airflow, fan or low-speed cooling problem.

Fan runs but car still overheats

Look deeper at coolant level, radiator restriction, thermostat, water pump or airlocks.

If the fan behaviour seems unusual, read radiator fan not working and car fan running after engine off.

Why it cools down when you start moving

If the temperature falls once you drive faster, the radiator may be getting enough airflow at road speed but not enough at idle. That does not mean the fault has gone away. It usually means moving air is temporarily helping the cooling system.

This pattern commonly points towards cooling fan operation, radiator airflow, coolant level, airlocks or poor low-speed circulation.

Warning signs that matter most

  • 1Temperature gauge rises quickly in queues.
  • 2Cooling fan never seems to run.
  • 3Steam or coolant smell appears.
  • 4Overheating warning light or coolant warning light appears.
  • 5Temperature improves once moving faster again.
  • 6Heater blows cold or coolant level drops.

What to check next

1. Watch when it happens

Only in traffic usually points towards low-speed cooling or fan operation.

2. Listen for the fan

The cooling fan should usually run when temperature rises or air conditioning is on.

3. Check coolant level

Only check when the engine is cool. Never open a hot coolant cap.

4. Look for leaks

Wet hoses, puddles, dried coolant residue or sweet smells may suggest coolant loss.

5. Inspect radiator area

Look for leaves, damage, blocked fins or obvious airflow restriction.

6. Arrange diagnosis

Repeated overheating should be checked before normal driving continues.

When to stop driving

Stop safely if the gauge enters the red, steam appears, the warning light comes on or the car starts running badly. Continued overheating can cause serious engine damage.

  • !Do not sit in traffic while the gauge keeps rising.
  • !Do not open the coolant cap while hot.
  • !Do not assume topping up coolant fixes the cause.
  • !Use recovery if overheating returns quickly.

Can air conditioning make overheating worse?

Air conditioning adds heat load to the front cooling pack. A healthy cooling system should cope, but if the radiator fan is weak, coolant is low or airflow is restricted, overheating may appear sooner with AC switched on.

If cooling problems appear with AC use, read car overheats with air conditioning on and car AC not blowing cold air.

Frequently asked questions

Why does my car only overheat in traffic?

Usually because airflow through the radiator is low and the cooling fan or cooling system is not coping.

Can a bad fan cause overheating?

Yes. A failed cooling fan, fuse, relay, wiring fault or fan control issue is one of the most common traffic overheating causes.

Why does it cool down when moving?

Road speed pushes air through the radiator, partly replacing the fan’s job.

Can low coolant cause this?

Yes. Low coolant can make overheating worse in traffic and at idle.

Should I turn the heater on?

It may temporarily remove some heat, but it is not a repair. Stop safely if the temperature continues to rise.

Should I keep driving?

No, not if the temperature keeps rising, steam appears or warning lights come on.
