Car Overheats in Traffic

If your car runs normally on open roads but overheats in slow traffic, queues or while stationary, the fault often involves low-speed cooling. Common causes include a failed radiator fan, low coolant, radiator restriction, thermostat faults or poor coolant circulation.

Quick answer

A car that overheats in traffic but cools down when moving is often struggling because there is not enough airflow through the radiator at low speed. The cooling fan, fan relay, radiator, coolant level and thermostat are common areas to check.

If the temperature gauge keeps rising, steam appears or a warning light comes on, stop safely and let the engine cool before checking anything.

Common causes of overheating in traffic

Radiator fan not working

The fan may fail to switch on when the car is stationary or moving slowly.

Low coolant level

Low coolant reduces heat transfer and can make traffic overheating worse.

Blocked radiator fins

Leaves, dirt or damaged fins can reduce airflow through the radiator.

Thermostat fault

A thermostat that does not open correctly can restrict coolant flow.

Weak water pump

Poor coolant circulation can show up at idle and low speed.

Hot weather and AC load

Warm weather, heavy traffic and air conditioning can expose a marginal cooling fault.

Why cooling fan faults show in traffic

When the car is moving, air naturally passes through the radiator. In traffic, that airflow drops, so the electric cooling fan must pull air through instead. If the fan, relay, fuse, wiring or temperature signal fails, the engine can get hot while stationary.

Fan never runs

Could be a fan motor, fuse, relay, wiring or sensor issue.

Fan runs constantly

May point to overheating, sensor faults or control issues.

Temperature drops when moving

Often supports an airflow or fan-related problem.

AC affects temperature

Air conditioning adds heat load and can make faults more obvious.

You may also find car fan running after engine off useful if the fan behaviour seems unusual.

Warning signs that matter most

  • 1Temperature gauge rises quickly in queues.
  • 2Cooling fan never seems to run.
  • 3Steam or coolant smell appears.
  • 4Overheating warning light appears.
  • 5Temperature improves once moving faster again.
  • 6Heater blows cold or coolant level drops.

What to check next

1. Watch when it happens

Only in traffic usually points towards low-speed cooling or fan operation.

2. Listen for the fan

The cooling fan should usually run when temperature rises or AC is on.

3. Check coolant level

Only check when the engine is cool. Never open a hot coolant cap.

4. Look for leaks

Wet hoses, puddles or sweet smells may suggest coolant loss.

5. Inspect radiator area

Look for leaves, damage or obvious airflow blockage.

6. Arrange diagnosis

Repeated overheating should be checked before normal driving continues.

When to stop driving

Stop safely if the gauge enters the red, steam appears, the warning light comes on or the car starts running badly. Continued overheating can cause serious engine damage.

  • !Do not sit in traffic while the gauge keeps rising.
  • !Do not open the coolant cap while hot.
  • !Do not assume topping up coolant fixes the cause.
  • !Use recovery if overheating returns quickly.

Can air conditioning make overheating worse?

Air conditioning adds heat load to the front cooling pack. A healthy cooling system should cope, but if the radiator fan is weak, coolant is low or airflow is restricted, overheating may appear sooner with AC switched on.

If cooling problems appear with AC use, read car AC not blowing cold air as well.

Frequently asked questions

Why does my car only overheat in traffic?

Usually because airflow is low and the cooling fan or cooling system is not coping.

Can a bad fan cause overheating?

Yes. A failed fan is one of the most common traffic overheating causes.

Why does it cool down when moving?

Road speed pushes air through the radiator, partly replacing the fan’s job.

Can low coolant cause this?

Yes. Low coolant can make overheating worse in traffic and at idle.

Should I turn the heater on?

It may temporarily remove some heat, but it is not a repair.

Should I keep driving?

No, not if the temperature keeps rising or warnings appear.
