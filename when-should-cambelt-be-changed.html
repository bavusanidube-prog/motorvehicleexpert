When should a cambelt be changed?

Cambelt replacement intervals vary by vehicle, engine and manufacturer. Many cars have both a mileage limit and an age limit, and ignoring either can be expensive because a failed cambelt can cause serious engine damage.

Quick answer

A cambelt should be changed at the interval recommended for the exact vehicle. This is usually based on mileage, age, or whichever comes first.

If the cambelt history is unknown, missing or overdue, it is safer to budget for replacement rather than assume it is fine.

Typical cambelt replacement intervals

There is no single rule for every car. Some cambelts may be due around 40,000 to 60,000 miles, while others may be closer to 80,000 to 100,000 miles or more. Age can matter just as much as mileage because rubber components deteriorate over time.

  • ✓Check the manufacturer schedule for the exact engine.
  • ✓Follow the age limit as well as the mileage limit.
  • ✓Use invoices as proof, not just a seller’s word.
  • ✓Replace early if history is unknown and the car is close to the interval.

Why cambelt timing matters

The cambelt keeps the engine’s internal timing synchronised. If it fails, the pistons and valves can collide on many engines, leading to major internal damage.

Unlike some faults, a cambelt can fail without much warning. That is why replacement is normally treated as scheduled maintenance rather than something you wait to hear or feel.

Mileage matters

High mileage increases belt, tensioner and pulley wear.

Age matters

A low-mileage cambelt can still be due because rubber ages.

History matters

No proof of replacement should be treated as no replacement.

What used car buyers should ask

  • 1Has the cambelt been replaced?
  • 2Is there an invoice with date, mileage and parts listed?
  • 3Was the tensioner replaced with the belt?
  • 4Was the water pump changed if recommended?
  • 5Is the next cambelt change already due soon?

Should the water pump be changed with the cambelt?

On many vehicles, the water pump is driven by the cambelt or sits in the same area. If it fails later, much of the same labour may be needed again.

For that reason, many garages recommend replacing the water pump, tensioner and related pulleys at the same time where suitable for the vehicle.

Best practical advice

Do not rely on visual appearance alone. A cambelt may look acceptable from the outside but still be due by age or mileage.

For a used car, missing cambelt proof should affect your buying decision. Either negotiate accordingly or budget for replacement immediately after purchase.

Frequently asked questions

Is mileage or age more important?

Both matter. Follow whichever limit comes first.

Can a cambelt look fine and still be due?

Yes. Visual condition is not a reliable way to judge cambelt service life.

What happens if a cambelt snaps?

On many engines, a snapped cambelt can cause serious valve, piston or cylinder head damage.

Is a cambelt the same as a timing belt?

Yes. Cambelt and timing belt are commonly used to describe the same part.

Do all cars have cambelts?

No. Some engines use timing chains instead, although chains can still have their own wear issues.
