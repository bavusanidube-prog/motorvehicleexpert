Acceleration fault guide

Car bucks when accelerating

If your car lurches, kicks or bucks forward when you try to accelerate, the cause is usually a drivability fault rather than normal performance behaviour. This guide explains common reasons a car bucks when accelerating and what drivers should do next.

Common causes

Why a car may buck under acceleration

Bucking usually means power delivery is arriving in sudden bursts instead of building smoothly and predictably.

Misfire

Misfire under load

A cylinder that is not firing properly can make the car lurch or kick during acceleration.

Fuel System

Uneven fuel delivery

If fuel flow is inconsistent, the engine may alternate between pulling and stumbling.

Sensor

Throttle or airflow sensor issue

Incorrect engine-management inputs can create harsh, jerky response when you press the accelerator.

Clutch

Clutch take-up or drivetrain harshness

Manual cars may feel like they buck if clutch engagement is poor or the drivetrain takes up drive unevenly.

Turbo or boost-control fault

Helpful clues

Signs that matter most

  • 1Only happens under heavier throttle
  • 2Feels worse in lower gears
  • 3Comes with hesitation or warning lights
  • 4Worse when the engine is cold
  • 5More obvious on hills or under load
What to do

Better next steps

1. Notice how the bucking starts

Light throttle, heavy throttle, hills or cold starts can all help narrow down the likely cause.

2. Check for other symptoms

Rough idle, warning lights, stuttering or poor fuel economy often point toward the right system more quickly.

3. Avoid random parts replacement

Bucking can come from fuel, ignition, sensors, boost or clutch-related faults, so guessing often wastes money.

4. Get it checked if it keeps happening

Intermittent drivability faults are usually easier to diagnose before they become more severe.
