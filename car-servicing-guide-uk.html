Car servicing guide UK: how often should you service your car?

Regular servicing helps keep your car reliable, safe and more economical to run. This guide explains service intervals, what is usually included, warning signs your car may need attention and how petrol, diesel and hybrid servicing can differ.

How often should you service a car in the UK?

As a general rule, most cars should be serviced every 12 months or at the manufacturer’s recommended mileage interval, whichever comes first. Many drivers follow an annual service routine because it keeps maintenance simple and helps spot problems early.

Your exact service schedule depends on the vehicle, engine type, mileage, age and how the car is used. Short journeys, stop-start traffic, towing, heavy loads and irregular use can all make more frequent servicing sensible.

What is usually included in a car service?

  • ✓Engine oil and oil filter replacement
  • ✓Air filter and pollen filter checks or replacement
  • ✓Brake pad, brake disc and brake fluid inspection
  • ✓Tyre tread depth, tyre pressure and tyre condition check
  • ✓Suspension, steering and wheel bearing inspection
  • ✓Battery health and charging system check
  • ✓Coolant, washer fluid, brake fluid and power steering fluid checks
  • ✓Lights, wipers, horn and screen wash operation
  • ✓Exhaust condition and visible leak inspection
  • ✓Diagnostic scan where warning lights or symptoms are present

Petrol car servicing

Petrol cars need regular oil and filter changes, spark plug checks, ignition system inspection and routine brake, tyre and fluid checks.

Diesel car servicing

Diesel cars may need extra attention around oil quality, fuel filters, turbo condition, EGR issues and DPF problems, especially if mainly used for short journeys.

Hybrid car servicing

Hybrid cars still need servicing. Brakes, tyres, fluids, suspension, cooling systems and safety checks remain important even if the engine runs less often.

Signs your car may need a service sooner

  • 1Engine oil warning light or low oil level
  • 2Brake noise, grinding, vibration or poor pedal feel
  • 3Engine management light or dashboard warning message
  • 4Poor fuel economy or loss of performance
  • 5Difficulty starting or repeated flat battery problems
  • 6Unusual knocking, rattling, whining or grinding noises

Interim service vs full service

An interim service is usually a smaller maintenance check suited to higher-mileage drivers or vehicles used heavily between annual services. It often includes an oil and filter change plus essential safety checks.

A full service is more detailed and usually includes wider checks of brakes, suspension, steering, tyres, filters, fluids, lights, battery and general vehicle condition. Your car may also need additional scheduled items such as spark plugs, brake fluid, coolant or timing belt replacement depending on age and mileage.

Why regular servicing matters

Reliability

Fresh oil, clean filters and regular checks reduce the risk of avoidable breakdowns.

Safety

Brakes, tyres, suspension and steering are checked before small faults become dangerous.

Fuel economy

A well-maintained engine usually runs more efficiently than a neglected one.

Resale value

A clear service history can make a used car easier to sell and more attractive to buyers.

Car servicing FAQs

Is a car service the same as an MOT?

No. An MOT is a legal roadworthiness test. A service is maintenance designed to keep the car reliable and reduce wear.

Can I drive if my service light is on?

A service reminder usually means scheduled maintenance is due. You may still be able to drive, but delaying servicing can increase wear and risk missing developing faults.

Do low-mileage cars still need servicing?

Yes. Oil, fluids, tyres, rubber components and batteries can deteriorate with time, not just mileage.

Should I service my car before an MOT?

It can be a good idea because a service may identify worn tyres, brake issues, lights, wipers or fluid problems before the MOT test.
