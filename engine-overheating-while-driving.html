Cooling system diagnostic guide

Car Overheats In Traffic

If your car overheats in traffic but cools down once moving again, the cooling system may struggle at low speed. In real life this often points to radiator fan faults, low coolant, trapped air, thermostat problems, blocked radiators or weak coolant circulation.

Quick answer

If a car overheats mostly in traffic or while idling, the problem is often linked to low-speed cooling performance rather than motorway airflow.

The radiator fan is one of the most common causes because the fan becomes important when the car is stationary or moving slowly. Low coolant, trapped air, thermostat faults and radiator restriction can also cause traffic overheating.

What this problem looks like in real life

Temperature rises at traffic lights

The gauge may slowly climb while stationary but return closer to normal once driving again.

Cooling fan never comes on

Many drivers notice the fan is silent even when the engine becomes very hot.

Why radiator fan faults are so common

At motorway speed, air naturally passes through the radiator. But in traffic, the cooling fan becomes essential because airflow drops dramatically.

If the fan fails, the engine temperature can rise surprisingly quickly while idling or crawling in traffic queues.

Signs the fan may not work properly

  • ✓Fan stays silent when hot.
  • ✓Temperature rises mostly at idle.
  • ✓Air conditioning performance drops.
  • ✓Temperature falls once moving.

  • ✓Faulty relay.
  • ✓Failed fan motor.
  • ✓Temperature sensor issue.
  • ✓Wiring or module fault.

When to stop driving immediately

  • !Temperature gauge reaches the red zone.
  • !Steam appears from under the bonnet.
  • !Coolant warning light appears.
  • !The heater blows cold while the engine is overheating.
  • !Coolant level becomes very low.
  • !The engine smells hot or loses power.

Stop safely, switch off the engine and allow it to cool fully before checking coolant level.

What to check first

1. Let engine cool properly

Never remove the coolant cap while hot because the system may be pressurised.

2. Check coolant level cold

Low coolant is one of the most important first checks.

3. Watch cooling fan operation

Allow the engine to warm up and see whether the radiator fan activates.

4. Look for leaks

Check hoses, radiator edges, expansion tank and underneath the vehicle.

5. Watch temperature behaviour

Note whether overheating happens only in traffic or also while driving normally.

6. Arrange proper diagnosis

Repeated overheating should always be pressure tested and diagnosed properly.

How garages diagnose traffic overheating

Cooling fan activation test

Checks whether the fan activates at the correct temperature.

Pressure testing

Helps identify coolant leaks and pressure loss.

Thermostat checks

Confirms coolant flow through the radiator.

Radiator temperature scanning

Cold spots can indicate internal blockage.

Water pump inspection

Checks for weak circulation or leaks.

Combustion gas testing

Helps rule out head gasket pressure problems.

Best mechanic-style advice

Do not ignore overheating that only happens in traffic. Many drivers continue driving because the temperature falls again once moving, but repeated overheating can still damage the engine over time.

In real workshop diagnostics, cooling fan operation, coolant level and pressure testing are usually checked early because they commonly reveal the root cause quickly.

Frequently asked questions

Why does my car overheat in traffic but not on the motorway?

This often points towards radiator fan problems, low-speed airflow issues, low coolant or trapped air in the cooling system.

Can a bad radiator fan cause overheating?

Yes. The radiator fan becomes important when the car is stationary or moving slowly.

Can low coolant cause overheating in traffic?

Yes. Low coolant reduces cooling efficiency and can introduce air into the system.

Why does the temperature go back down while driving?

Moving airflow through the radiator helps cool the engine naturally at higher speed.

Should I drive if the temperature gauge enters the red?

No. Stop safely and allow the engine to cool before checking anything.

Can air conditioning make overheating worse?

Yes. Air conditioning adds heat load and can expose cooling fan or radiator problems.

Can trapped air cause overheating in traffic?

Yes. Airlocks can interrupt coolant circulation and create unstable temperatures.

Can repeated overheating damage the engine?

Yes. Repeated overheating can damage the head gasket, cylinder head and cooling system components.
