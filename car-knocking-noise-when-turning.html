Car Knocking Noise When Turning

A knocking noise when turning can come from worn suspension parts, CV joints, steering components, strut top mounts, wheel bearings, anti-roll bar links or loose wheel fittings. The timing, side and type of noise help narrow down the cause.

Quick answer

If your car knocks when turning, common causes include worn CV joints, suspension bushes, ball joints, strut top mounts, track rod ends, anti-roll bar links, wheel bearings or loose wheel fittings.

A repeated knock should not be ignored because steering, suspension and wheel faults can worsen and may affect safety, tyre wear, handling and MOT results.

Common causes of knocking when turning

CV joint wear

Often causes clicking or knocking on full lock, especially while accelerating slowly.

Suspension bushes

Worn bushes can knock as the car changes direction or loads one side.

Ball joint wear

May cause clunks, loose steering feel, uneven tyre wear or MOT failure.

Strut top mount

Can pop, creak or knock as the steering turns.

Track rod end wear

Can cause knocking, steering play, poor alignment or uneven tyre wear.

Anti-roll bar link

Often knocks over bumps and can be more noticeable while turning.

Wheel bearing issue

Usually causes humming or rumbling, but turning can change the noise.

Loose wheel fitting

A loose wheel, damaged nut or poor fitting can be dangerous and needs urgent checking.

Tyre scrub

Some low-speed scrubbing on full lock can sound unusual but may not be a fault.

Knocking noise on full lock

A knocking or clicking noise on full lock often points toward an outer CV joint, especially on front-wheel-drive cars. It may be louder when pulling out of a parking space, turning tightly at low speed or accelerating with the steering fully turned.

  • ✓Noise is worse when accelerating while turning.
  • ✓Repeated clicking or knocking comes from one front corner.
  • ✓There is a split CV boot or grease around the wheel area.
  • ✓The noise is more obvious in car parks or tight manoeuvres.

Low-speed knocking when parking or manoeuvring

Knocking at low speed can be caused by suspension joints, steering links, strut top mounts, anti-roll bar links or loose components. These parts move most when the car is turning, braking, crossing uneven ground or shifting weight from one side to the other.

Knock over bumps

Often points toward anti-roll bar links, bushes, ball joints or suspension arms.

Knock while steering stationary

Can suggest a strut top mount, steering rack, track rod end or power steering-related issue.

Knock when braking and turning

Can point towards suspension arm movement, bushes, loose brake parts or wheel fitting issues.

Knock after hitting a pothole

Have the wheel, tyre, suspension and steering checked for impact damage.

Knocking when turning left or right

The direction of the turn can help identify which side is loaded. For example, turning left often loads the right side of the car, while turning right often loads the left side. However, noise can travel through the body, so the source is not always obvious from the driver’s seat.

A garage road test and ramp inspection is usually the best way to confirm whether the fault is a CV joint, suspension joint, steering part, wheel bearing or loose fitting.

What to check first

  • 1Notice whether it happens on full lock, gentle turns, bumps or braking.
  • 2Check whether the noise comes from the left, right, front or rear.
  • 3Look for split CV boots or grease around the inner wheel area.
  • 4Check for uneven tyre wear, steering vibration or pulling.
  • 5Check wheel nuts if the noise started after wheel or tyre work.
  • 6Do not ignore knocking that gets louder, more frequent or affects steering feel.
  • 7Arrange a front suspension, steering and wheel inspection if the noise repeats.

Can knocking when turning affect MOT?

Yes. The knocking itself is a symptom, but the cause may be an MOT issue. Worn suspension joints, excessive play, broken springs, insecure mountings, loose steering parts, wheel bearing play or unsafe wheel fittings can all cause MOT problems.

Can you drive with a knocking noise when turning?

If the noise is occasional and light, the car may still drive, but it should be checked soon. Steering and suspension noises often worsen over time, especially if caused by play in a joint or damaged rubber boot.

Avoid driving if the steering feels loose, the car pulls strongly, the noise is loud, the wheel area feels unstable, the car has recently hit a pothole hard, or you suspect a loose wheel. A loose wheel or badly worn steering/suspension component can be dangerous.

Frequently asked questions

Why does my car knock when turning left?

It may be a loaded suspension, CV joint, steering or wheel component on one side. The direction can help a mechanic narrow it down.

Why does it knock on full lock?

Full-lock knocking often points toward CV joint wear, especially if it clicks repeatedly while moving slowly.

Can suspension bushes knock?

Yes. Worn bushes can knock as the suspension moves and loads shift during cornering.

Is a knocking noise dangerous?

It can be. Steering, suspension and wheel faults should be inspected if the noise repeats or worsens.

Can wheel bearings knock when turning?

Wheel bearings more often hum or rumble, but turning can change the sound if a bearing is worn.

Should I get it checked before MOT?

Yes. Knocking can point to MOT-relevant steering, suspension or wheel defects.
