Can Wheel Alignment Fail MOT?

Wheel alignment itself is not normally a direct MOT measurement item, but the symptoms and causes of poor alignment can lead to failure. Uneven tyre wear, worn steering joints, damaged suspension parts or unsafe handling are common examples.

Quick answer

Poor wheel alignment alone does not usually fail an MOT because testers do not normally carry out a full alignment measurement during the test.

However, if poor alignment has caused severe tyre wear, pulling, worn steering parts or suspension defects, the vehicle may fail on those related items.

How poor alignment can lead to MOT issues

Uneven tyre wear

Inner or outer edge wear may leave tyres below legal limits.

Pulling to one side

May indicate steering, tyre or suspension faults.

Off-centre steering wheel

Often a sign of tracking or geometry issues.

Track rod wear

Loose steering joints can fail the MOT.

Lower arm wear

Bushes or joints may alter alignment angles.

Suspension damage

Bent or worn components can be unsafe.

Tyre wear caused by bad alignment

One of the most common MOT risks from poor alignment is abnormal tyre wear. Even if tread looks acceptable in the centre, the inner or outer shoulders may be worn out.

Inside edge wear

Often linked to toe or camber problems and easy to miss without turning the steering.

Outside edge wear

Can happen with incorrect geometry or frequent cornering stress.

Feathered tread

Tyre blocks feel sharp one way and smooth the other.

Rapid wear

Misalignment can shorten tyre life significantly.

Signs wheel alignment may be out

  • !Steering wheel not straight while driving straight.
  • !Car drifts left or right on a level road.
  • !Rapid tyre shoulder wear.
  • !Steering feels vague or unstable.
  • !Recent pothole strike or kerb impact.
  • !New suspension or steering parts recently fitted.

What to check before your MOT

1. Inspect tyre edges

Check inner and outer shoulders for heavy wear.

2. Check steering position

See if the wheel sits straight when driving normally.

3. Listen for knocks

Noises over bumps may suggest worn suspension joints or bushes.

4. Check tyre pressures

Incorrect pressures can mimic alignment symptoms.

5. Review advisories

Previous tyre, steering or suspension advisories can be clues.

6. Book inspection early

A garage can inspect parts before setting alignment.

Should you fix alignment before the MOT?

Yes, especially if tyres are wearing unevenly or the car pulls noticeably. Correct alignment can help prevent tyre failure items and improve handling.

If alignment cannot be set correctly, that often suggests worn or bent steering or suspension parts that should be repaired first.

Can potholes knock alignment out?

Yes. Hitting potholes or kerbs can alter tracking, damage tyres or bend steering and suspension components.

If symptoms start after an impact, have the vehicle checked promptly.

Frequently asked questions

Is wheel alignment tested on MOT?

Not usually as a direct measurement item.

Can tyres fail from bad alignment?

Yes, if tread wear becomes excessive or unsafe.

Should I align after suspension repair?

Usually yes, especially after steering or suspension work.

Can potholes affect alignment?

Yes, impacts commonly knock geometry out.

Will pulling to one side fail MOT?

Not automatically, but the cause may be a fail item.

Best next step before MOT?

Inspect tyres and book steering or suspension checks if symptoms exist.
