Used Car Inspection Checklist UK

Quick answer

Before buying a used car, check the paperwork, MOT history, service records, mileage pattern, VIN, tyres, bodywork, accident repair signs, warning lights, engine condition and test-drive behaviour.

The biggest red flags are missing documents, mismatched details, warning lights, overheating, smoke, major leaks, clocked-mileage clues, poor accident repairs, repeated MOT advisories and a seller who rushes or avoids direct questions.

Before viewing the car

Before travelling to see a used car, check the advert carefully. Look for clear photos, matching details, sensible mileage, MOT status and whether the seller’s description sounds honest.

  • 1Check the registration number against the MOT history.
  • 2Compare the mileage in the advert with MOT mileage records.
  • 3Ask whether the car has service history, invoices and proof of major work.
  • 4Check whether the car is being sold privately, by a trader or by a dealer.
  • 5Ask if there are any known faults before travelling.
  • 6Consider a vehicle history check for finance, theft or write-off status.
  • 7Be cautious if the price is far cheaper than similar cars without a clear reason.
  • 8Arrange to view the car in daylight, ideally when the engine is cold.

Important documents to check

Paperwork does not prove a car is perfect, but poor paperwork can reveal risk. The details on the documents should match the car and the seller’s story.

  • ✓The V5C logbook should match the registration, VIN and keeper details.
  • ✓The VIN on the car should match the V5C and any history report.
  • ✓Service history should include invoices, stamps or digital records where possible.
  • ✓MOT history should show consistent mileage and no worrying repeat advisories.
  • ✓Check how many keys are included and whether replacements are expensive.
  • ✓Check proof of cambelt, timing chain, clutch, DPF or major repairs if claimed.
  • ✓Keep proof of payment and a written receipt if you buy the car.

How to spot possible clocked mileage

Mileage alone does not tell the whole story, but it should make sense when compared with MOT history, service invoices, vehicle condition and wear inside the cabin.

MOT mileage gaps

Look for sudden drops, odd jumps, missing years or mileage that does not match the advert.

Interior wear

Heavy seat, steering wheel, gear knob or pedal wear can look suspicious on a supposedly low-mileage car.

Service record mismatch

Invoices should show dates, mileage and work carried out. Missing records around key years deserve caution.

Condition mismatch

Stone chips, worn tyres, tired suspension or shiny interior controls may not match very low mileage claims.

If mileage seems suspicious, walk away or pay for a professional inspection and history check before committing.

Exterior checks

  • 1Check body panels for dents, rust, scratches and poor paint repairs.
  • 2Look for uneven panel gaps, which may suggest previous accident damage.
  • 3Inspect tyres for low tread, cracks, bulges, uneven wear and mixed budget tyres.
  • 4Check all lights, indicators, mirrors, wipers and washers.
  • 5Look underneath for oil, coolant, fuel or brake fluid leaks.
  • 6Check windscreen chips, number plates and visible MOT-related issues.
  • 7Look for corrosion on sills, wheel arches, subframes and suspension mounting areas.
  • 8Check tyre date codes and whether all four tyres match reasonably.

Tyre, light and windscreen issues can affect MOT results. See common MOT failure reasons UK.

How to spot accident repair or poor bodywork

Not every repaired car is bad, but poor accident repairs can hide alignment, suspension, corrosion and resale-value problems.

Uneven panel gaps

Doors, bonnet, boot and bumper gaps should look even from side to side.

Paint mismatch

Different shades between panels can suggest previous paintwork.

Overspray

Look around rubber seals, arch liners, trims and inside door shuts.

Fresh underseal

Fresh coating can be innocent, but it can also hide corrosion or repairs.

Misaligned lights

Headlights and rear lights that sit unevenly can suggest previous impact.

Pulling on test drive

A car that pulls, shakes or feels unstable may have alignment, tyre or suspension issues.

Interior and dashboard checks

  • ✓Check seat wear, steering wheel wear and pedal wear against the mileage.
  • ✓Make sure windows, central locking, mirrors and infotainment work.
  • ✓Check the heater, air conditioning and fan speeds.
  • ✓Turn the ignition on and make sure warning lights illuminate, then go out after starting.
  • ✓Check for damp carpets, bad smells or signs of water leaks.
  • ✓Look under mats and in the boot for moisture, mould, rust or water staining.
  • ✓Make sure the spare wheel, locking wheel nut and tools are present if supplied.
  • ✓Check seatbelts retract properly and are not frayed or damaged.

Dashboard warnings matter. Use the warning lights guide if any light stays on after the engine starts.

Signs of possible flood damage

Flood-damaged cars can suffer long-term electrical, corrosion and interior problems. Be cautious if the car smells damp or has unexplained electrical faults.

  • !Damp carpets, mould smell or water staining under mats.
  • !Condensation inside lights or windows that keeps returning.
  • !Rust on seat rails, brackets, wiring connectors or under the dashboard.
  • !Electrical faults affecting windows, locks, lights, sensors or infotainment.
  • !Fresh interior cleaning smell that seems to hide damp or mould.
  • !Silt, grit or mud in boot corners, spare wheel well or under trim.

Engine bay checks

You do not need to be a mechanic to spot basic warning signs. A clean engine bay is not proof of a good car, but obvious leaks, low fluids or poor repairs should make you cautious.

  • 1Check oil level and look for thick sludge under the oil cap.
  • 2Check coolant level and look for signs of leaks or overheating.
  • 3Look for cracked hoses, loose belts or obvious fluid leaks.
  • 4Listen for rattles, knocking, misfires or uneven idle after startup.
  • 5Watch for smoke from the exhaust when starting and accelerating.
  • 6Check for burning oil, petrol, coolant or exhaust smells.
  • 7Check whether the engine starts cleanly from cold.
  • 8Look for warning signs of head gasket, coolant loss or overheating issues.

Checks during the test drive

A test drive is one of the best ways to uncover faults that are not obvious while parked. Try to include town driving, gentle acceleration, braking, gear changes and a short faster section where legal and safe.

  • ✓The engine should pull smoothly without hesitation, smoke or warning lights.
  • ✓The clutch should not slip, judder or smell burnt.
  • ✓Gears should select smoothly without crunching or delay.
  • ✓Brakes should stop straight without grinding, vibration or pulling.
  • ✓Steering should feel stable without shaking, drifting or heavy spots.
  • ✓Suspension should not knock loudly over bumps.
  • ✓The temperature gauge should stay stable once warm.
  • ✓No warning lights should appear during the drive.

Buying from a dealer vs private seller

Dealer purchase

Usually offers more consumer protection than a private sale, but still inspect the car carefully and check what warranty is actually included.

Private purchase

Can be cheaper, but risk is higher. Check the seller’s identity, address, V5C details, payment method and whether the car is genuinely theirs to sell.

Trader pretending to be private

Be cautious if the seller has multiple cars for sale, avoids paperwork questions or will not meet at the registered address.

Warranty wording

Read the exclusions. A “warranty” may not cover wear-and-tear, diagnosis, labour limits or pre-existing faults.

Used car buying red flags

  • !The seller refuses a test drive or independent inspection.
  • !The V5C is missing or seller details do not match.
  • !The VIN does not match the paperwork or looks tampered with.
  • !The mileage does not match MOT or service records.
  • !There are warning lights, overheating, smoke or major leaks.
  • !The seller rushes you or avoids direct questions.
  • !The car has repeated MOT advisories for the same issue.
  • !The price is unusually low and the explanation does not add up.
  • !The seller asks for unusual payment arrangements or deposit pressure.

Common used car scams and traps

Outstanding finance

A history check can help reveal finance that may not be obvious from the advert.

Clocked mileage

Mileage may be altered to increase the car’s value. Compare MOT, service and condition clues.

Hidden write-off

A vehicle history check can reveal recorded insurance write-off categories.

Fake private seller

Some traders pretend to be private sellers to avoid responsibilities.

Deposit pressure

Be cautious if pushed to send money before seeing the car properly.

Fresh MOT trap

A fresh MOT is useful, but it does not prove the car has been serviced or is fault-free.

When to walk away

Sometimes the best buying decision is not negotiating harder — it is leaving the car alone. Walk away if the risk is unclear, the seller is evasive or the car has symptoms you cannot afford to repair.

  • !The car overheats or loses coolant during viewing or test drive.
  • !Engine management, airbag, ABS or oil warnings stay on.
  • !There is blue smoke, heavy white smoke or strong fuel smell.
  • !The gearbox slips, bangs, delays or refuses gears.
  • !The seller will not let you verify documents or inspect properly.
  • !Your history checks do not match the seller’s story.

Frequently asked questions

What should I check before buying a used car?

Check the documents, MOT history, service history, mileage, VIN, tyres, bodywork, engine bay, warning lights and how the car drives.

Is 100k miles too much for a used car?

Not always. Condition, servicing and previous maintenance matter more than mileage alone. Read our 100k miles used car guide.

Should I buy a used car without a test drive?

Usually no. A test drive can reveal clutch, gearbox, brake, steering, suspension and engine faults that are not obvious while parked.

Can MOT history reveal problems?

Yes. Repeated advisories, mileage gaps, corrosion notes and recurring faults can suggest poor maintenance.

Should I get a professional inspection?

For expensive cars, high-mileage cars, performance cars, diesels or anything you are unsure about, an independent inspection can be worthwhile.

Is a fresh MOT enough when buying a used car?

No. A fresh MOT is useful, but it is not the same as a service, warranty or full mechanical inspection.

What is the biggest used car red flag?

Missing paperwork, mismatched VIN, warning lights, smoke, overheating, mileage inconsistencies and a seller refusing inspection are all major red flags.
