Used Car Inspection Checklist UK

Buying a used car can save money, but hidden faults can quickly become expensive. Use this UK used car inspection checklist to check documents, MOT history, bodywork, tyres, engine condition, warning lights, test drive behaviour and seller red flags before you buy.

Before viewing the car

Before travelling to see a used car, check the advert carefully. Look for clear photos, matching details, sensible mileage, MOT status and whether the seller’s description sounds honest.

  • 1Check the registration number against the MOT history.
  • 2Compare the mileage in the advert with MOT mileage records.
  • 3Ask whether the car has service history and invoices.
  • 4Check whether the car is being sold privately or by a dealer.
  • 5Ask if there are any known faults before travelling.
  • 6Consider a vehicle history check for finance, theft or write-off status.

Important documents to check

  • ✓The V5C logbook should match the registration, VIN and seller details.
  • ✓Service history should include invoices, stamps or digital records where possible.
  • ✓MOT history should show consistent mileage and no worrying repeat advisories.
  • ✓Check how many keys are included.
  • ✓Check the VIN on the vehicle matches the paperwork.
  • ✓Keep proof of payment and a written receipt if you buy the car.

For more detail, read our guides to checking MOT history before buying and questions to ask when buying a used car.

Exterior checks

  • 1Check body panels for dents, rust, scratches and poor paint repairs.
  • 2Look for uneven panel gaps, which may suggest previous accident damage.
  • 3Inspect tyres for low tread, cracks, bulges and uneven wear.
  • 4Check all lights, indicators, mirrors, wipers and washers.
  • 5Look underneath for oil, coolant, fuel or brake fluid leaks.
  • 6Check windscreen chips, number plates and visible MOT-related issues.

Tyre, light and windscreen issues can also affect MOT results. See our common MOT failure reasons UK guide.

Interior and dashboard checks

  • ✓Check seat wear, steering wheel wear and pedal wear against the mileage.
  • ✓Make sure windows, central locking, mirrors and infotainment work.
  • ✓Check the heater, air conditioning and fan speeds.
  • ✓Turn the ignition on and make sure warning lights illuminate, then go out after starting.
  • ✓Check for damp carpets, bad smells or signs of water leaks.
  • ✓Make sure the spare wheel, locking wheel nut and tools are present if supplied.

Engine bay checks

You do not need to be a mechanic to spot basic warning signs. A clean engine bay is not proof of a good car, but obvious leaks, low fluids or poor repairs should make you cautious.

  • 1Check oil level and look for thick sludge under the oil cap.
  • 2Check coolant level and look for signs of leaks or overheating.
  • 3Look for cracked hoses, loose belts or obvious fluid leaks.
  • 4Listen for rattles, knocking, misfires or uneven idle after startup.
  • 5Watch for smoke from the exhaust when starting and accelerating.
  • 6Check for burning oil, petrol, coolant or exhaust smells.

Checks during the test drive

A test drive is one of the best ways to uncover faults that are not obvious while parked.

  • ✓The engine should pull smoothly without hesitation, smoke or warning lights.
  • ✓The clutch should not slip, judder or smell burnt.
  • ✓Gears should select smoothly without crunching.
  • ✓Brakes should stop straight without grinding, vibration or pulling.
  • ✓Steering should feel stable without shaking or drifting.
  • ✓Suspension should not knock loudly over bumps.

Used car buying red flags

  • !The seller refuses a test drive or inspection.
  • !The V5C is missing or seller details do not match.
  • !The mileage does not match MOT or service records.
  • !There are warning lights, overheating, smoke or major leaks.
  • !The seller rushes you or avoids direct questions.
  • !The car has repeated MOT advisories for the same issue.

Frequently asked questions

What should I check before buying a used car?

Check the documents, MOT history, service history, tyres, bodywork, engine bay, warning lights and how the car drives.

Is 100k miles too much for a used car?

Not always. Condition, servicing and previous maintenance matter more than mileage alone. Read our 100k miles used car guide.

Should I buy a used car without a test drive?

Usually no. A test drive can reveal clutch, gearbox, brake, steering and engine faults that are not obvious while parked.

Can MOT history reveal problems?

Yes. Repeated advisories, mileage gaps and recurring faults can suggest poor maintenance.

Should I get a professional inspection?

For expensive cars, high-mileage cars or anything you are unsure about, an independent inspection can be worthwhile.
