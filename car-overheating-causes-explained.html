Cooling system diagnosis guide

Car Overheating Causes Explained

If your car temperature rises above normal, steam appears, coolant disappears or the heater suddenly blows cold, the cooling system is not removing heat properly. In real workshop situations, overheating is usually linked to coolant loss, leaks, thermostat faults, radiator fan problems, blocked radiators, water pump failure, trapped air or internal engine pressure issues.

Mechanic tip: overheating rarely “fixes itself”. A car that overheats once should always be monitored carefully because repeated overheating can quickly become expensive engine damage.

Quick answer

Cars overheat because the cooling system cannot move enough heat away from the engine. The most common causes are low coolant, coolant leaks, poor coolant circulation, blocked radiator airflow, thermostat faults, radiator fan failure, weak water pumps, trapped air and internal engine pressure problems.

Overheating should always be taken seriously. Driving while the gauge is high or in the red can damage the head gasket, cylinder head, engine oil and cooling-system components.

Common overheating warning signs

  • !Temperature gauge rising higher than normal.
  • !Coolant warning light appears.
  • !Steam from under the bonnet.
  • !Heater suddenly blowing cold.
  • !Sweet coolant smell after driving.
  • !Coolant level repeatedly dropping.
  • !Coolant bubbling in expansion tank.
  • !White smoke from exhaust with coolant loss.

Car overheats in traffic

If the temperature rises mainly while sitting in traffic or stationary, the cooling fan becomes one of the first things to check. At low speed there is less natural airflow through the radiator.

Typical clue

Gauge rises while stopped but drops once driving faster again.

Likely causes

Fan motor, relay, fuse, airflow restriction, low coolant or circulation problem.

Engine overheating at idle

Idle overheating often points towards cooling fan operation or weak coolant circulation because the engine is generating heat without strong airflow through the radiator.

Fan never starts

Possible electrical, sensor or relay issue.

Fan runs constantly

Possible temperature sensor or cooling-system issue.

Heater changes temperature

Can suggest airlocks or coolant circulation problems.

Read more: engine overheating when idle.

Engine overheating while driving

Overheating while driving or on the motorway usually points more towards coolant flow problems than airflow problems.

Typical clue

Temperature rises under load, uphill or at motorway speed.

Likely causes

Thermostat restriction, blocked radiator, water pump weakness or severe coolant loss.

Coolant loss and overheating

Coolant loss is one of the strongest overheating clues. A cooling system should not need repeated top-ups.

  • ✓Sweet coolant smell after driving.
  • ✓Puddles or coloured residue around hoses or radiator.
  • ✓Coolant warning light appearing repeatedly.
  • ✓Coolant disappearing with no obvious leak.
  • ✓Heater blowing cold while engine runs hot.

Could overheating be a head gasket problem?

Not every overheating car has a blown head gasket, but repeated overheating combined with coolant loss, bubbling coolant or white smoke needs proper testing.

Bubbling coolant

May indicate trapped gases or boiling coolant.

White smoke

Persistent white smoke can suggest coolant entering the combustion chamber.

Repeated overheating

Cars that repeatedly overheat after topping up need proper diagnosis.

What to check first

1. Let the engine cool

Never open the coolant cap while hot.

2. Check coolant level

Low coolant is one of the strongest overheating clues.

3. Look for leaks

Check hoses, radiator, expansion tank and underneath the car.

4. Watch the heater

Cold heater plus overheating often points to coolant circulation problems.

5. Notice when overheating happens

Traffic, idle and motorway overheating patterns help narrow the cause.

6. Arrange diagnosis

Repeated overheating needs proper cooling-system testing.

When to stop driving immediately

  • !Temperature gauge enters the red.
  • !Steam comes from under the bonnet.
  • !Coolant warning light stays on.
  • !Coolant is leaking heavily.
  • !Heater blows cold while engine gets hotter.
  • !Engine loses power or runs roughly.

Best mechanic-style advice

Do not guess parts based on overheating alone. The pattern matters: traffic overheating, idle overheating, motorway overheating, coolant loss, sweet smells, cold heater behaviour and bubbling coolant all point in different directions.

Start with coolant level, leak checks, fan operation and heater behaviour before assuming the worst.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most common cause of car overheating?

Low coolant or coolant loss is one of the most common causes, followed by fan faults, thermostat issues and coolant circulation problems.

Can I drive if my car is overheating?

It is risky. Stop driving if the gauge rises high, steam appears or the coolant warning light stays on.

Why does my car overheat in traffic?

Traffic overheating often points to radiator fan problems, airflow issues or coolant circulation faults.

Why does my heater blow cold while overheating?

This often happens when coolant is low or coolant circulation is poor.

Can low coolant cause overheating?

Yes. Low coolant reduces the system's ability to remove heat properly.

Can a thermostat cause overheating?

Yes. A thermostat stuck closed can stop coolant circulating properly.

Can a blocked radiator cause overheating?

Yes. Blocked or damaged radiators reduce cooling efficiency and airflow.

Can a water pump cause overheating?

Yes. Weak coolant circulation from a failing pump can cause overheating.

Can a head gasket cause repeated overheating?

Yes. Internal pressure and coolant loss can create repeated overheating problems.

Should I just keep topping up coolant?

No. Repeated coolant loss means the fault still needs diagnosing properly.
