Brake Warning Light On While Driving

A brake warning light that appears while driving should never be ignored. It may be caused by low brake fluid, worn brake components, a parking brake switch fault, ABS-related issues or a more serious hydraulic braking problem.

Quick answer

If the brake warning light comes on while driving, treat it seriously. Some causes are simple, such as a parking brake switch issue, but others can affect your ability to stop safely.

If the brake pedal feels soft, travel increases, the car pulls when braking, braking feels weak or you see fluid leaking, stop driving as soon as it is safe and arrange recovery or professional inspection.

What does the brake warning light mean?

The brake warning light can mean different things depending on the car. It may relate to the parking brake, low brake fluid, brake pad wear, hydraulic brake pressure, ABS or another braking system fault.

Red brake warning

Usually more urgent, especially if linked to fluid level, brake pressure or braking performance.

Amber brake warning

Often indicates a fault that needs checking soon, such as pad wear or an electronic brake issue.

ABS light also on

May mean anti-lock braking or stability systems are affected as well.

Common reasons the brake warning light comes on

Low brake fluid

Can be linked to worn brake pads or a leak in the braking system.

Parking brake issue

The handbrake may not be fully released, or the switch may be faulty.

Worn brake pads

Some vehicles trigger a warning when brake pads are close to replacement.

Hydraulic leak

Brake fluid loss can reduce braking performance and become dangerous quickly.

ABS or stability fault

Brake warnings can appear with ABS, traction control or stability control lights.

Sensor or wiring fault

Electrical faults can trigger warnings even when the mechanical brakes seem normal.

When you should stop driving immediately

  • !The brake pedal feels soft, spongy or sinks towards the floor.
  • !Stopping distance increases or braking feels weak.
  • !The car pulls sharply to one side when braking.
  • !You hear grinding noises or feel severe vibration.
  • !You can see brake fluid leaking near a wheel or under the car.
  • !The brake warning light appears with other warning lights.

If any of these happen, stop somewhere safe and do not continue driving. Braking faults can become dangerous without much warning.

What to check first

1. Check the parking brake

Make sure the handbrake or electronic parking brake is fully released.

2. Notice the brake feel

Pedal feel matters more than the light alone. Soft, sinking or weak brakes are urgent.

3. Check brake fluid level

Only check if it is safe. Low fluid should always be investigated rather than ignored.

4. Look for leaks

Check for wet patches near the wheels, under the car or around brake components.

5. Check for other warning lights

ABS, traction control or engine warnings can help narrow down the fault.

6. Arrange inspection

Brake warnings should be diagnosed promptly, especially if they return after restarting.

Brake fluid low: what it may mean

Low brake fluid may happen because brake pads have worn down over time, but it can also indicate a leak. A leak is more serious because it can reduce hydraulic pressure and braking ability.

Topping up the fluid without finding the reason is not a proper fix. If the level drops again, the car needs urgent inspection.

Can a brake warning light affect MOT?

Yes. Brake system defects, warning lights and poor braking performance can affect the MOT result. If a brake warning light is on before the test, it is better to diagnose it first rather than risk a failure.

Frequently asked questions

Can I keep driving with the brake warning light on?

Only if braking feels completely normal and you are driving carefully to a safe place for inspection. If the pedal feels soft, braking is weak or fluid is leaking, stop driving.

Can worn brake pads trigger the brake warning light?

Yes, on some vehicles. Worn pads can also lower the fluid level slightly as the caliper pistons move further out.

What if the brake pedal feels soft?

Stop driving and arrange help immediately. A soft or sinking pedal can indicate air, fluid loss or hydraulic failure.

Could it just be the handbrake switch?

Sometimes, but do not assume that until braking performance and fluid level have been checked.

Will a brake warning light fail MOT?

It can, depending on the fault. Brake defects and warning lights should be investigated before the test.

What is the best next step?

Check whether braking feels normal, look for obvious leaks, confirm the parking brake is released and arrange a proper brake inspection.
