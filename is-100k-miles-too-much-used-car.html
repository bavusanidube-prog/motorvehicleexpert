Used car buying guide

Is 100,000 miles too much for a used car?

A used car with 100,000 miles is not automatically a bad buy. Mileage matters, but condition, service history, ownership, repair evidence, MOT history and how the car has been driven usually matter more than the number alone.

What to check Back to guides
Quick answer

Is 100k miles too much?

Not automatically. A well-maintained 100,000-mile car can be a better purchase than a neglected low-mileage car. The key is whether the car has been serviced properly, repaired sensibly and looked after throughout its life.

Condition beats mileage

A clean, well-maintained high-mileage car can be a sensible buy if the history supports it.

History matters

Service records, MOT history and repair invoices help show whether the car has been looked after.

Price must make sense

High mileage should usually be reflected in the asking price compared with lower-mileage examples.

Buying checklist

What to check on a 100k-mile car

  • 1Full or believable service history
  • 2Timing belt, timing chain or major maintenance evidence
  • 3Clutch condition, especially on manual cars
  • 4Brake, tyre, suspension and wheel bearing condition
  • 5Engine leaks, smoke, overheating signs or warning lights
Good signs

When high mileage is less worrying

Regular servicing

Consistent servicing and oil changes are a strong positive sign.

Motorway use

A car used mostly for longer journeys may have had an easier life than one used only for short trips.

Careful ownership

Fewer careful owners and good paperwork usually make a car easier to judge.

Recent major work

Evidence of clutch, cambelt, brakes, tyres or suspension work can reduce near-term costs.

Walk away?

When a 100k-mile car is risky

  • 1No service history or very patchy records
  • 2Several dashboard warning lights
  • 3Smoke, overheating, rough running or serious leaks
  • 4Poorly repaired accident damage or suspicious paintwork
  • 5Seller avoids basic questions or rushes the sale
Extra checks

Costs that often appear around higher mileage

Around 100,000 miles, some cars may need larger maintenance items depending on the model and history. Common areas to check include clutch wear, timing belt intervals, suspension parts, brakes, tyres, alternator, starter motor, wheel bearings and cooling system condition.

The important point is not whether the car has reached 100k miles, but whether these items have already been maintained or whether you may be buying the next owner’s repair bills.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Is 100k miles bad for a petrol car?

Not automatically. A petrol car with 100,000 miles can still be a sensible buy if it has been serviced properly and drives well.

Is 100k miles bad for a diesel car?

Not automatically. Diesel cars can suit longer journeys, but check DPF history, servicing, turbo condition and how the car has been used.

Is service history more important than mileage?

Usually yes. Mileage matters, but service history often tells you much more about how the car has been cared for.

Bottom line

Should you buy a 100k-mile used car?

Yes, if the price is fair, the history is strong, the condition is good and the car passes a sensible inspection and test drive.

Be more cautious if the car has missing history, warning lights, rough running, smoke, overheating, poor repairs or signs that expensive maintenance has been avoided.
