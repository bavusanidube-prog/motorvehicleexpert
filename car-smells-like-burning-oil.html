Burning smell guide

Car Smells Like Burning Oil

If your car smells like burning oil, engine oil may be leaking or spilling onto hot parts such as the exhaust, manifold or engine casing. It can also be linked to low oil level, overheating, recent servicing or age-related gasket leaks.

Quick answer

A burning oil smell usually means oil is reaching a hot surface. A small amount of spilled oil after servicing may burn off, but repeated smells, smoke, oil warning lights or dropping oil level should be checked quickly.

Do not ignore a strong burning oil smell. Oil leaks can worsen, create smoke, contaminate components and leave the engine low on oil.

Common reasons a car smells like burning oil

Oil leak onto exhaust

Oil dripping onto hot exhaust parts is one of the most common causes.

Rocker cover gasket leak

Oil can seep from the top of the engine and burn on hot surfaces.

Spilled oil after service

Oil spilled during a top-up or oil change may smell briefly after driving.

Low oil level

Low oil can cause engine stress and warning lights alongside unusual smells.

Overheating engine

Excess heat can make oil, grime and residues smell stronger.

Turbo oil leak

Some turbo faults can create burning oil smells or smoke from the exhaust.

Oil filter or sump plug leak

Recent servicing can expose loose fittings or sealing problems.

Old engine seals

Older vehicles can develop seepage from worn gaskets and seals.

Oil burning internally

Blue smoke may suggest oil is being burned inside the engine.

Warning signs that need attention

  • !Smoke from under the bonnet.
  • !Oil warning light appears or flickers.
  • !Oil level keeps dropping between checks.
  • !Blue smoke from the exhaust.
  • !Burning smell after every drive.
  • !Engine temperature rises higher than normal.

Burning oil smell after a service or oil change

A brief burning oil smell after an oil change can happen if a small amount of oil was spilled onto hot engine parts. This should fade after a short time.

If the smell continues, check for leaks around the oil filter, sump plug, filler cap and any areas disturbed during the service. A persistent smell after maintenance should be reported to the garage.

What to check next

1. Check the oil level

Use the dipstick or dashboard reading if your vehicle has one.

2. Look under the car

Fresh oil spots can suggest an active leak.

3. Inspect the engine bay

Look for wet oily residue around the engine, filter, filler cap and hoses.

4. Watch for smoke

Smoke under the bonnet or blue exhaust smoke changes the urgency.

5. Check warning lights

Oil, engine or temperature warnings should not be ignored.

6. Arrange diagnosis

A garage can confirm whether it is spilled oil, seepage or a serious leak.

Frequently asked questions

Why does my car smell like burning oil?

Most commonly, oil is leaking or spilling onto hot engine or exhaust parts.

Can I drive with a burning oil smell?

Avoid driving if there is smoke, warning lights or low oil level. Mild smells should still be checked if repeated.

Is it normal after an oil change?

A brief smell can happen from spilled oil, but it should not continue for long.

Can low oil cause a smell?

Low oil can cause engine stress and warning lights, but the smell often comes from leaks or hot residue.

What does blue smoke mean?

Blue smoke can suggest oil is being burned inside the engine and should be checked.

Best first step?

Check oil level, look for leaks and avoid driving if smoke or warning lights appear.
