Exhaust Smoke Colour Guide

Exhaust smoke colour can give useful clues about engine health. Thin white vapour on a cold morning is often normal, but thick white, blue, black or grey smoke can point to coolant loss, oil burning, excess fuel, turbo faults or emissions problems.

Quick answer: what does exhaust smoke colour mean?

A small amount of light vapour from the exhaust on a cold start is usually condensation. It should clear as the engine warms up. Smoke that is thick, coloured, persistent, smells strong or appears with warning lights should not be ignored.

White smoke

Often condensation when thin. Thick white smoke can suggest coolant entering the engine.

Blue smoke

Usually means engine oil is being burned.

Black smoke

Usually means too much fuel, not enough air or diesel emissions problems.

Grey smoke

Can be oil, fuel, turbo or PCV related and needs diagnosis if persistent.

White exhaust smoke

Thin white vapour after starting the car is often just condensation, especially in cold or damp weather. This should fade once the exhaust system warms up.

Thick white smoke that keeps coming from the exhaust after the engine is warm is more serious. It can suggest coolant is entering the combustion chamber, which may be linked to a head gasket fault, cracked cylinder head or internal engine problem.

  • ✓Normal condensation in cold weather
  • ✓Coolant entering the cylinders
  • ✓Head gasket failure
  • ✓Cracked cylinder head or engine damage
  • ✓Coolant level dropping regularly

If white smoke appears with overheating or coolant loss, read car overheating — what to do and coolant leak repair cost UK.

Blue exhaust smoke

Blue smoke usually means the engine is burning oil. It may appear when starting the car, accelerating, idling or after coasting downhill, depending on the cause.

  • ✓Worn piston rings allowing oil into the cylinders
  • ✓Worn valve stem seals
  • ✓Turbocharger oil seal faults
  • ✓Blocked crankcase ventilation or PCV system
  • ✓Too much engine oil after a top-up or service

Blue smoke should be checked promptly because running low on oil can cause major engine damage. If an oil warning light appears, see oil warning light on and off.

Black exhaust smoke

Black smoke usually means the engine is burning too much fuel or not getting enough air. It is more common on diesel vehicles, but petrol cars can also produce black smoke if the fuel mixture is too rich.

  • ✓Dirty or blocked air filter
  • ✓Injector problems or over-fuelling
  • ✓Mass airflow sensor or oxygen sensor faults
  • ✓EGR valve or intake issues
  • ✓DPF or diesel emissions system faults
  • ✓Poor fuel economy and loss of power

If the car is diesel and a DPF warning light is on, read DPF warning light explained. If the car also feels weak, see car losing power when accelerating.

Grey exhaust smoke

Grey smoke can be harder to diagnose because it may look like blue or black smoke depending on the light and driving conditions. Persistent grey smoke should still be taken seriously.

  • ✓Turbocharger wear or oil leakage
  • ✓PCV or crankcase ventilation faults
  • ✓Minor oil burning
  • ✓Fuel mixture problems
  • ✓Automatic transmission fluid issue on some older vehicles

Grey smoke with a burning smell may overlap with other smell symptoms. See car smells like burning oil and car smells hot after driving.

When the smoke appears matters

The colour of the smoke is important, but the timing can be just as useful. Make a note of when it happens before speaking to a garage.

Only at startup

May be condensation, valve stem seal wear or oil settling overnight.

Under acceleration

Can point to turbo faults, fuelling problems, injector issues or engine wear.

At idle

May suggest oil burning, fuel mixture issues, PCV faults or cooling system problems.

After coasting

Blue smoke after slowing down can suggest valve stem seal wear.

Constant smoke

Usually points to an ongoing mechanical, fuel or emissions fault.

Only during regeneration

Some diesel smells or vapour can appear during DPF regeneration, but heavy smoke is not normal.

When exhaust smoke is serious

Stop driving and get help if the smoke is heavy, the engine overheats, the oil or coolant level drops quickly, or the car loses power. These symptoms can lead to expensive engine or turbo damage.

  • !Smoke becomes thick, constant or gets worse quickly
  • !Coolant level drops or the engine overheats
  • !Oil level falls quickly or oil warning light appears
  • !Engine management light comes on
  • !Vehicle loses power, misfires or runs roughly
  • !Strong burning, fuel or exhaust smell develops

If a warning light is showing, read engine management light guide.

Can exhaust smoke fail an MOT?

Yes. Excessive exhaust smoke, high emissions, visible diesel smoke or faults with emissions systems can cause MOT problems. A car can also fail if the smoke suggests a serious defect or if the engine warning light relates to emissions.

If your car has already failed or is due for a test, see car fails MOT on emissions, can an exhaust leak fail MOT? and common MOT failure reasons UK.

What to do next

1. Note the colour

White, blue, black and grey smoke point to different likely causes.

2. Notice when it happens

Cold start, idle, acceleration, motorway driving or constant smoke all matter.

3. Check oil and coolant

Do not ignore falling fluid levels, overheating or oil warning lights.

4. Book diagnosis

A scan, smoke test, compression test or cooling system pressure test may be needed.

Frequently asked questions

Is white smoke always bad?

No. Thin white vapour in cold weather is often normal condensation. Thick white smoke after warm-up is more serious.

Does blue smoke always mean oil burning?

Usually yes. Blue smoke normally means oil is entering the combustion process and should be checked promptly.

Can black smoke damage the engine?

It can. Black smoke may mean over-fuelling, poor combustion or emissions system problems, which can damage parts over time.

Can exhaust smoke fail an MOT?

Yes. Excessive smoke or emissions faults can cause MOT failure.

Should I drive with smoke coming from the exhaust?

Light condensation on a cold start is usually fine. Heavy, coloured, persistent or smelly smoke should be checked, especially with warning lights or poor running.
