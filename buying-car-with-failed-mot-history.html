Buying a Car With Failed MOT History

A failed MOT history does not automatically mean a bad used car. Many cars fail on simple items such as tyres, bulbs or wipers and are repaired quickly. What matters is the pattern of failures, whether repairs were done properly and how the car looks and drives today.

Should you buy a car with failed MOT history?

Yes, sometimes. Old MOT failures are not always a problem if the car was repaired properly and now has a clean test.

Be more cautious if the same problems appear repeatedly, especially corrosion, brakes, suspension, steering, tyres, emissions or mileage concerns.

When MOT failures may be normal

Bulbs and lights

Usually simple fixes, unless the same lighting faults appear repeatedly.

Tyres

Tyres are wear items, but repeated tyre neglect may suggest poor maintenance.

Wipers and washers

Often low-cost maintenance items rather than major mechanical problems.

Brake pads

Brake pads wear out naturally, but repeated brake failures deserve closer checks.

Single old failure

One old failure is usually less worrying than a pattern over several years.

Quick retest pass

A quick pass after failure can suggest the repair was dealt with promptly.

Failure patterns that matter more

The most important thing is the pattern. A car that fails every year for neglect-related items may have been maintained only when forced by the MOT.

  • !Repeated corrosion or structural rust mentions.
  • !Frequent brake failures or poor braking performance.
  • !Suspension, steering or wheel bearing faults year after year.
  • !Emissions failures, smoke or engine management problems.
  • !Mileage inconsistencies or unexplained MOT gaps.

Red flags before buying

  • !The seller dismisses repeated failures as “nothing”.
  • !The MOT history shows corrosion getting worse each year.
  • !Recent failure items have no repair evidence.
  • !The car has current warning lights, smoke, leaks or poor braking.
  • !The mileage does not match MOT records or service history.
  • !The seller refuses inspection or a proper test drive.

What to inspect now

Do not rely only on old MOT results. Check the car’s current condition carefully before buying.

  • 1Check tyres, brakes, suspension and steering for wear or noises.
  • 2Look underneath for corrosion, oil leaks and damaged components.
  • 3Check dashboard warning lights go out after startup.
  • 4Take a proper test drive on different road types.
  • 5Ask for repair invoices for previous MOT failures.
  • 6Compare MOT history with service records and current condition.

Frequently asked questions

Is it bad if a car failed an MOT in the past?

Not always. Many cars fail on minor items and are repaired quickly. Repeated serious failures are more concerning.

Should I avoid a car with failed MOT history?

Avoid it if failures suggest poor maintenance, corrosion, mileage issues or unresolved safety problems.

Is a quick MOT retest pass a good sign?

It can be. A quick retest pass may show the faults were fixed promptly, but you should still inspect the current condition.

Can failed MOT history help me negotiate?

Yes, especially if the failures suggest current or future repair costs.

What MOT failures are most concerning?

Corrosion, braking problems, steering faults, suspension defects, emissions failures and repeated safety-related issues deserve extra caution.
