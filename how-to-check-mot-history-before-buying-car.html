How To Check MOT History Before Buying A Used Car

Checking MOT history before buying a used car is one of the quickest ways to spot trouble before wasting money. In real life, the MOT record can reveal mileage problems, repeated advisories, rust, tyre wear, brake faults, suspension issues and cars that only get repaired when they fail. Use it before viewing, before test driving and definitely before paying a deposit.

Quick answer

Use the car’s registration number to check MOT history before buying. Look at previous failures, advisories, mileage records, rust notes, brake issues, tyre wear, suspension problems and whether the same faults appear year after year.

A clean MOT history does not prove the car is perfect, but a poor MOT pattern can warn you about neglect, future repair bills, suspicious mileage and sellers trying to move a problem car on.

Why MOT history matters when buying a used car

The MOT record gives you a timeline. It does not tell the whole story, but it shows how the car has performed at annual tests and whether faults were allowed to repeat.

Mileage records

MOT history can help show whether mileage has increased normally over time.

Recurring faults

Repeated advisories can suggest ongoing neglect or repairs being delayed.

Corrosion clues

Rust advisories can become expensive, especially on older vehicles.

Brake and tyre care

Frequent tyre, brake or suspension advisories may show weak maintenance habits.

Negotiation help

MOT history can help you discuss price if repairs are likely soon.

Buying confidence

Consistent passes with few advisories can support a better buying decision.

How to check MOT history before viewing

  • 1Get the registration number from the advert or seller.
  • 2Check the MOT test history before arranging a viewing.
  • 3Compare the MOT mileage with the advert mileage.
  • 4Look at failures, advisories and whether repairs repeat.
  • 5Make notes of anything you want to inspect in person.
  • 6Ask the seller direct questions before travelling.
  • 7Use the MOT history alongside service records and a test drive.

What to check in MOT history

  • 1Previous MOT failures and whether they were repaired properly.
  • 2Repeated advisories appearing year after year.
  • 3Mileage progression that looks sensible and consistent.
  • 4Rust, corrosion or structural advisories.
  • 5Brake, tyre, steering and suspension patterns.
  • 6Recent failures just before the car was listed for sale.
  • 7Dangerous defects or major defects in recent tests.
  • 8Whether the car seems repaired properly or just patched up for the test.

How to spot mileage concerns

MOT history records mileage at each test. The mileage should usually rise steadily each year. A sudden drop, strange jump or mileage that does not match the advert deserves more questions.

  • !Mileage goes backwards between MOT tests.
  • !Large gaps in MOT history without explanation.
  • !Advert mileage does not match recent MOT mileage.
  • !Interior wear looks much worse than the mileage suggests.
  • !Service invoices show mileage that does not match the MOT pattern.

How to read MOT advisories

One advisory is not always a problem. Patterns matter more. If the same tyre, brake, suspension or corrosion issue appears repeatedly, it may suggest the owner has delayed repairs.

One-off advisory

Can be normal on an older car, especially if it was repaired soon after.

Repeated advisory

Suggests the same issue has been left for more than one test cycle.

Safety advisory

Brake, steering, tyre and suspension notes should be treated seriously.

Corrosion advisory

Rust can spread and may become expensive if it reaches structural areas.

For more detail, read MOT advisory meaning explained and should you buy a car with advisories?

Should you worry about failed MOT history?

A past failed MOT does not automatically mean the car is bad. What matters is why it failed, how serious the fault was, whether it was repaired properly and whether similar faults keep returning.

Less worrying

Old failure, simple fault, repaired properly, no repeated pattern and clean later tests.

More worrying

Recent failure, dangerous defects, rust, brakes, steering, suspension or repeated failures.

Rust and corrosion in MOT history

Rust advisories deserve proper attention, especially on older cars. Surface rust and structural corrosion are not the same thing, so check the wording and inspect the car underneath.

  • !Corrosion near suspension mounting points.
  • !Rust on sills, subframes, arches or structural areas.
  • !Repeated corrosion advisories over several years.
  • !Fresh underseal that may hide rust or poor repairs.
  • !Rust advisories that suddenly disappear without repair evidence.

MOT history red flags before buying

  • !Repeated failures for similar faults.
  • !Heavy corrosion or structural rust mentioned more than once.
  • !Frequent tyre, brake or suspension advisories.
  • !Several recent failures shortly before sale.
  • !Mileage inconsistencies or unexplained gaps.
  • !Seller dismisses serious advisories as “nothing”.
  • !Fresh MOT but no service history or repair invoices.
  • !Repeated emissions failures on a diesel or smoky car.

Before you buy the car

MOT history is useful, but it should not be your only check. Combine it with service history, physical inspection, seller questions and a proper test drive.

  • 1Check MOT history before viewing.
  • 2Ask the seller about repeated advisories or failures.
  • 3Compare MOT mileage with the advert and service records.
  • 4Inspect the car carefully in daylight.
  • 5Test drive long enough to check brakes, steering, gearbox and temperature.
  • 6Walk away if the history, condition and seller story do not match.

Best mechanic-style advice

Do not read MOT history like a pass or fail only. Read it like a maintenance diary. The pattern matters more than one line.

A car with repeated tyre, brake, rust and suspension notes may have been run on the cheap. A car with a fresh MOT but no service history still needs checking properly.

Frequently asked questions

Can MOT history prove a car is good?

No. MOT history is useful evidence, but it does not replace service history, physical inspection or a proper test drive.

Is one failed MOT a bad sign?

Not always. One old failure may be fine if it was repaired properly. Repeated failures and unresolved patterns are more concerning.

Should I avoid a car with MOT advisories?

Not always. Minor advisories can be normal, but repeated or safety-related advisories should affect your decision and price.

Can MOT history show mileage problems?

Yes. MOT mileage records can reveal unusual jumps, drops or inconsistencies that need explaining.

Should I check MOT history before viewing?

Yes. It can save wasted journeys and help you ask better questions before seeing the car.

Is a fresh MOT enough when buying a used car?

No. A fresh MOT only proves the car passed on the day. It does not prove the car has been serviced properly or has no hidden faults.

What MOT advisories are most serious?

Corrosion, brakes, steering, tyres, suspension and structural advisories should be taken seriously, especially if they appear more than once.

Can MOT history show clocked mileage?

It can show signs that need checking, such as mileage drops, odd jumps or readings that do not match the advert or service records.

Should I ask for invoices after MOT failures?

Yes. Repair invoices help prove whether the previous failures or advisories were properly fixed.

When should I walk away?

Walk away if mileage does not add up, rust looks serious, failures repeat, the seller avoids questions or the car’s condition does not match its MOT story.
