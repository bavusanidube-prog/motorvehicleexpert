How To Check MOT History Before Buying a Car

Checking MOT history before buying a used car can reveal past failures, repeated advisories, mileage concerns and signs of poor maintenance. It is one of the easiest ways to spot potential problems before you travel, test drive or pay a deposit.

Quick answer: what should you check?

Use the car’s registration number to check its MOT history before buying. Look at previous failures, advisories, mileage records, corrosion notes and whether the same faults appear year after year.

A clean MOT history does not guarantee a perfect car, but it helps you judge whether the vehicle has been maintained properly or only repaired when it fails.

Why MOT history matters when buying a used car

Mileage records

MOT history can help show whether mileage has increased normally over time.

Recurring faults

Repeated advisories can suggest ongoing neglect or repairs being delayed.

Corrosion clues

Rust advisories can become expensive, especially on older vehicles.

Brake and tyre care

Frequent tyre, brake or suspension advisories may show weak maintenance habits.

Negotiation help

MOT history can help you discuss price if repairs are likely soon.

Buying confidence

Consistent passes with few advisories can support a better buying decision.

What to check in MOT history

  • 1Previous MOT failures and whether they were repaired properly.
  • 2Repeated advisories appearing year after year.
  • 3Mileage progression that looks sensible and consistent.
  • 4Rust, corrosion or structural advisories.
  • 5Brake, tyre, steering and suspension patterns.
  • 6Recent failures just before the car was listed for sale.

How to spot mileage concerns

MOT history records mileage at each test. The mileage should usually rise steadily each year. A sudden drop, unusual gap or mileage that does not match the advert deserves more questions.

  • !Mileage goes backwards between MOT tests.
  • !Large gaps in MOT history without explanation.
  • !Advert mileage does not match recent MOT mileage.
  • !Interior wear looks much worse than the mileage suggests.

How to read MOT advisories

One advisory is not always a problem. Patterns matter more. If the same tyre, brake, suspension or corrosion issue appears repeatedly, it may suggest the owner has delayed repairs.

  • ✓Minor one-off advisories may be normal on older cars.
  • !Repeated advisories can suggest poor maintenance.
  • !Corrosion advisories should be checked carefully in person.
  • !Brake, steering and suspension advisories may affect safety and cost.

MOT history red flags before buying

  • !Repeated failures for similar faults.
  • !Heavy corrosion or structural rust mentioned more than once.
  • !Frequent tyre, brake or suspension advisories.
  • !Several recent failures shortly before sale.
  • !Mileage inconsistencies or unexplained gaps.
  • !Seller dismisses serious advisories as “nothing”.

Before you buy the car

MOT history is useful, but it should not be your only check. Combine it with a physical inspection, service history review and proper test drive.

  • 1Check the MOT history before viewing.
  • 2Ask the seller about any repeated advisories or failures.
  • 3Compare MOT mileage with the advert and service records.
  • 4Inspect the car carefully in person.
  • 5Walk away if the history, condition and seller story do not match.

Frequently asked questions

Can MOT history prove a car is good?

No. It is useful evidence, but it does not replace service history, physical inspection or a test drive.

Is one failed MOT a bad sign?

Not always. One old failure may be fine if it was repaired properly. Repeated failures and unresolved patterns are more concerning.

Should I avoid a car with MOT advisories?

Not always. Minor advisories can be normal, but repeated or safety-related advisories should affect your decision and price.

Can MOT history show mileage problems?

Yes. MOT mileage records can reveal unusual jumps, drops or inconsistencies that need explaining.

Should I check MOT history before viewing?

Yes. It can save wasted journeys and help you ask better questions before seeing the car.
