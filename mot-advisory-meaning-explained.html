MOT Advisory Meaning Explained

An MOT advisory means the car has passed its MOT, but the tester has noted something that may need attention soon. Advisories are not immediate test failures, but they should never be treated as meaningless because they often point to wear, deterioration or issues that may become more serious later.

Quick Answer

What does an MOT advisory mean?

An MOT advisory is a note recorded during the MOT test to highlight a defect, wear issue or condition that is not serious enough to fail the vehicle at that time, but could need attention soon.

In simple terms, your car has passed, but the tester is warning you that something may need monitoring, maintenance or repair.

Why it matters

Why advisories should not be ignored

Many drivers focus only on whether the car passed or failed. That can be a mistake. An advisory may be the early warning sign of a future MOT failure, a repair bill or a developing safety issue.

If left too long, a minor advisory can become a much more expensive problem by the next test.

Common examples

Typical MOT advisories drivers see

Advisory items vary depending on the condition of the vehicle, but some come up more often than others.

Tyres wearing low

Tyres may still be legal, but the tester is noting wear before they become too low.

Brake wear

Brake pads or discs may be worn but not yet below the MOT limit.

Suspension wear

Bushes, dampers or joints may show wear that should be monitored.

Corrosion noted

Rust may not yet be severe enough for failure, but it has been identified as a concern.

Minor leaks

Fluid leaks may be present at a level that does not yet fail the test.

Worn components

General wear may be recorded on steering, braking or underbody parts.

How serious is it?

Not all advisories are equal

Some advisories are low priority and simply worth monitoring. Others are strong warnings that a part is likely to need attention soon. The wording matters.

For example, an advisory noting “slightly worn” may be less urgent than one stating a component is “worn close to limit” or “deteriorated.”

Good next step

What to do after an advisory

  • 1Read the wording carefully
  • 2Prioritise anything safety-related
  • 3Get a garage opinion if the wording is unclear
  • 4Fix issues before they become failures
Buying a used car

Why MOT advisories matter when buying

Advisories can tell you a lot about how a car has been maintained. A repeated pattern of the same advisory year after year may suggest neglect, while a clean advisory history can be a reassuring sign.

Repeated tyre advisories

May suggest poor maintenance habits or money being spent only when necessary.

Corrosion advisories

Worth checking carefully, especially on older cars or vehicles used in harsh conditions.

Suspension and brake notes

These may point to future repair costs even if the car passed the test.

FAQ

Common questions about MOT advisories

Does an advisory mean the car failed?

No. An advisory means the car passed, but the tester noted an issue worth attention.

Can you ignore an MOT advisory?

You can, but it is usually unwise. Some advisories turn into failures or bigger repair bills later.

Are advisories serious?

Sometimes. Some are minor, but others are early warnings of safety-related or costly future problems.

Should I fix advisories straight away?

Safety-related advisories should be prioritised. Others may be monitored, but should still be planned for.
