MOT Advisory Meaning Explained

An MOT advisory means your car has passed its MOT, but the tester has noticed something worth monitoring or repairing. Advisories are not immediate MOT failures, but they can point to tyre wear, brake wear, suspension play, corrosion, leaks or future repair costs.

Quick answer

An MOT advisory means the car passed the test, but the tester noticed an issue that may need attention soon. It is not a fail, but it should not be treated as meaningless.

The most important advisories are usually linked to tyres, brakes, steering, suspension, corrosion, fluid leaks and warning lights. These can become failures later and may affect safety or repair costs.

If you are buying a used car, advisories help show how the car has been looked after. Repeated advisories for the same issue can suggest neglect or upcoming bills.

What does an MOT advisory mean?

An MOT advisory is a note recorded by the tester when they spot something that is not serious enough to fail the vehicle at the time of the test.

In simple terms, the car has passed, but the tester is warning you that something may need monitoring, maintenance or repair before it becomes worse.

The car passed

An advisory does not mean an MOT failure.

The issue was noticed

The tester saw wear, deterioration or a possible future concern.

You should monitor it

Some advisories become future failures if ignored.

Are MOT advisories serious?

Some advisories are minor and can be monitored. Others are early warnings of safety-related faults or future MOT failures.

  • 1Low priority: slight wear, minor corrosion or early deterioration.
  • 2Medium priority: tyres wearing low, brake wear, light suspension wear or small leaks.
  • 3High priority: anything affecting brakes, steering, tyres, suspension, corrosion, leaks or road safety.

The exact wording matters. “Slightly worn” is usually less urgent than “worn close to limit”, “deteriorated”, “corroded” or “leaking”.

Common MOT advisory examples

Tyres wearing low

The tyres may still be legal, but they are getting close to needing replacement.

Brake wear

Brake pads or discs may be worn but not yet bad enough to fail.

Suspension wear

Bushes, joints, springs or shock absorbers may show signs of wear.

Corrosion noted

Rust may not yet be severe, but it should be watched carefully.

Minor leaks

Oil, coolant or power steering leaks may be present but not severe enough to fail.

Wipers, lights or washers

Small items can become MOT failures if ignored before the next test.

Advisory wording clues: what the words usually suggest

MOT advisory wording can give you a clue about urgency. Do not only look at the number of advisories — read what they actually say.

“Slightly worn”

Usually means early wear. Monitor it and ask when it may need replacement.

“Worn close to limit”

More urgent. The item may soon become a failure or safety concern.

“Corroded”

Needs careful checking, especially if rust is near brakes, suspension, sills or structural areas.

“Leaking”

Leaks can worsen quickly. Identify the fluid and source before it becomes expensive.

“Deteriorated”

Often means rubber, metal or mounting condition is declining and may need future repair.

Repeated wording every year

Repeated advisories show the issue has been left unresolved and may be getting worse.

What should you do after an MOT advisory?

  • ✓Read the advisory wording carefully instead of just counting how many there are.
  • ✓Prioritise anything related to tyres, brakes, steering, suspension, corrosion or leaks.
  • ✓Ask a garage how urgent the issue is if you are unsure.
  • ✓Plan repairs before the advisory becomes a failure.
  • ✓Keep receipts if you fix the issue, especially when selling the car later.
  • ✓Check old MOT history to see whether the same advisory appears repeatedly.

Why MOT advisories matter when buying a used car

MOT advisories can reveal how a car has been maintained. One advisory is not always a problem, but repeated advisories for the same issue may suggest neglect.

  • !Repeated tyre advisories may suggest poor maintenance habits.
  • !Repeated brake advisories may point to upcoming repair bills.
  • !Corrosion advisories should be checked carefully on older cars.
  • !Suspension advisories can become expensive if several parts are worn.
  • !Oil leaks, coolant leaks or steering leaks should not be ignored.
  • !Advisories that disappear without repair evidence can still deserve inspection.

Advisory vs minor vs major defect

Advisory

The car passes, but an issue has been noted for monitoring or future repair.

Minor defect

A defect exists but is not serious enough to fail the MOT. It should still be repaired.

Major or dangerous defect

The car fails the MOT and the issue needs repair before a valid pass is issued.

Best mechanic-style advice

Do not ignore advisories just because the car passed. MOT advisories are often the tester telling you, “This is not a fail today, but it is heading that way.”

If the advisory affects tyres, brakes, steering, suspension, corrosion or leaks, treat it as a repair planning warning rather than a note to forget.

Frequently asked questions

Does an MOT advisory mean the car failed?

No. An advisory means the car passed, but the tester noticed an issue worth monitoring or repairing.

Can I ignore an MOT advisory?

You can legally continue driving if the car passed, but ignoring advisories can lead to future failures, repair bills or safety issues.

Should I fix MOT advisories straight away?

Safety-related advisories such as tyres, brakes, steering and suspension should be prioritised. Less urgent items may be monitored, but should not be forgotten.

Are MOT advisories bad when buying a used car?

Not always. A few minor advisories may be normal on an older car, but repeated or serious advisories should affect your decision and the price.

Do MOT advisories show online?

Yes. MOT history usually shows previous advisories, failures and mileage records, which can help you judge how well a car has been maintained.

Can an advisory become a failure next year?

Yes. Wear, corrosion, leaks, tyre condition and brake issues can get worse and become MOT failures later.

Should I buy a car with many MOT advisories?

Only with caution. Check what the advisories are, whether they repeat, how expensive they may be and whether the price reflects the risk.

What advisories are most urgent?

Tyres, brakes, steering, suspension, corrosion, leaks and safety-related advisories should be treated as the highest priority.
