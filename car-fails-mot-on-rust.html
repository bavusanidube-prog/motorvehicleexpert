MOT corrosion guide

Car Fails MOT on Rust

If your car fails its MOT on rust, the corrosion is usually affecting a structural, safety-related or prescribed area. Light surface rust may pass, but serious corrosion near suspension mounts, brakes, steering, seatbelts, sills, jacking points or chassis sections often needs proper repair before the car can pass.

Mechanic tip: rust is judged by location and severity, not just appearance. A small hole in the wrong place can be more serious than a large patch of surface rust on a non-structural panel.

Quick answer

Rust usually fails an MOT when it weakens the vehicle structure, creates holes in important areas, affects suspension or steering mountings, compromises seatbelt anchorages, damages brake pipes, or creates sharp edges that could injure someone.

Small cosmetic rust bubbles or light surface rust do not automatically mean failure. The key questions are where the corrosion is, how severe it is, and whether it affects safety, strength, braking, steering, suspension or occupant restraint.

Surface rust vs structural rust

Surface rust

Surface rust is usually light corrosion on the outer layer of metal. It may look untidy, but it may not fail an MOT if the metal is still strong and the area is not safety-critical.

Structural rust

Structural rust weakens metal, creates holes, flakes away in layers or affects load-bearing areas. This is where MOT failures become more likely.

Cosmetic panel rust

Rust on doors, wings or wheel arches may be cosmetic unless it creates sharp edges or affects safety-related areas.

Hidden rust

Rust behind underseal, plastic covers, sill trims or carpets can be worse than it first appears. Hidden corrosion often increases repair cost once work starts.

Common areas where rust can fail MOT

Sills

Inner and outer sills are common corrosion areas, especially near jacking points and seatbelt zones.

Chassis sections

Rust affecting chassis rails, floor strength or load-bearing areas can fail.

Suspension mounts

Corrosion around suspension mounting points is safety-critical.

Seatbelt mounts

Seatbelt anchorages must remain strong, secure and free from serious corrosion.

Brake pipes

Floorpan holes

Holes may fail depending on severity, sharp edges and proximity to safety-related areas.

Subframes

A badly corroded subframe may need replacement if strength or mounting security is affected.

Jacking points

Rotten jacking points can indicate sill or structural corrosion nearby.

Sharp body edges

Rust holes or jagged metal can fail if they create a risk of injury.

Why rust near certain parts is more serious

Rust becomes more serious when it is close to parts that carry load, restrain occupants, hold suspension or keep the car controlled. This is why a small rusty patch near a seatbelt mounting, suspension point or brake pipe can matter more than rust on a cosmetic outer panel.

  • !Near seatbelts: the metal must be strong enough to hold the occupant in a crash.
  • !Near suspension mounts: weakness can affect wheel control and vehicle stability.
  • !Near steering parts: corrosion around steering mountings can be safety-critical.
  • !Near brake pipes: corrosion can lead to leaks or sudden brake failure.
  • !On load-bearing structure: weak metal can reduce crash strength and roadworthiness.

Major vs dangerous rust defects

Major corrosion defect

The car fails the MOT and needs repair before a pass certificate can be issued. The defect is serious enough to affect MOT roadworthiness.

Dangerous corrosion defect

The car is considered a direct safety risk and should not be driven until repaired, even if the previous MOT had not yet expired.

If rust has weakened a structural area or mounting point, do not assume the vehicle is safe just because it still drives normally. Rust often gets serious before the driver feels anything from behind the wheel.

Can rust MOT failures be repaired?

  • 1Welding repairs are common for structural rust.
  • 2Replacement panels or repair sections may be needed.
  • 3Hidden corrosion can make the job larger once cutting begins.
  • 4Repairs must restore strength, not just cover the hole.
  • 5Poor plating, filler or underseal over weak metal may still fail.
  • 6A retest is normally needed after repair.

Rust repairs that may fail again

A proper MOT rust repair should restore the strength of the affected area. Covering rotten metal with filler, thick underseal or a cosmetic patch may look better briefly but can still fail if the structure underneath is weak.

Filler over holes

Body filler is not a structural repair and should not be used to hide serious corrosion.

Underseal over rot

Underseal can protect good metal, but it should not be used to disguise weak metal.

Patching without cutting rot

Welding over rotten metal can leave weak areas behind and may not last.

No cavity protection

Fresh welded areas can rust again if the inside is not protected properly.

Poor brake pipe routing

Brake pipe repairs must be secure, correctly routed and safe.

Ignoring nearby rust

Fixing one hole while leaving nearby corrosion can lead to another failure soon.

Is it worth fixing a car that failed MOT on rust?

It depends on the car’s value, mileage, service history, overall mechanical condition and how widespread the corrosion is. A small local repair on a good car may be worthwhile. Widespread rust on a low-value car can become uneconomical quickly.

Before approving repairs, ask whether there is hidden corrosion, whether more welding may be found, and whether the finished repair is likely to last more than one MOT cycle.

More likely worth repairing

The car is mechanically good, mileage is reasonable, rust is localised and repair cost is sensible compared with replacement cost.

May not be worth repairing

Rust is widespread, structural repairs are extensive, previous repairs are failing or the repair quote is close to the car’s value.

What happens next after a rust MOT failure?

  • 1Read the MOT failure sheet and identify the exact corrosion location.
  • 2Ask the garage whether the defect is major or dangerous.
  • 3Get a repair quote from a garage or welder experienced with MOT corrosion repairs.
  • 4Check whether hidden rust may increase the quote once work starts.
  • 5Keep repair invoices and photographs if available.
  • 6Arrange the MOT retest after the corrosion repair is complete.

For retest timing and rules, read MOT retest rules UK. If the failure sheet also lists advisories, read MOT advisory meaning explained.

How to prevent rust MOT failures

1. Treat advisories early

Corrosion advisories often become failures if ignored for another year.

2. Wash underneath

Removing winter salt and dirt can slow corrosion on older cars.

3. Check sills and jacking points

These areas often rust before the rest of the body looks bad.

4. Inspect brake pipes

Heavily corroded pipes are a common safety-related MOT problem.

5. Be cautious with underseal

Underseal can protect good metal, but it should not hide weak or holed metal.

6. Check MOT history

Repeated corrosion advisories are a warning sign when buying a used car.

Rust checks when buying a used car

Rust can turn a cheap used car into an expensive repair very quickly. Before buying, check MOT history carefully and look underneath the vehicle, not just at the paintwork.

  • ✓Check MOT history for repeated corrosion advisories or failures.
  • ✓Look at sills, jacking points, floor edges and wheel arches.
  • ✓Be cautious if fresh underseal hides older corrosion.
  • ✓Check brake pipes for heavy corrosion advisories.
  • ✓Look for poor welding patches or thick filler around sills.
  • ✓Ask whether previous corrosion repairs have invoices or photos.

Useful buying guides: used car inspection checklist, how to check MOT history before buying and buying a car with failed MOT history.

Best mechanic-style advice

Do not judge rust only from the outside. A sill can look acceptable from above but be rotten underneath. A car can also have clean paint and still have serious corrosion behind plastic covers or under old underseal.

If the rust failure is structural, get a proper quote before approving work. Ask what will be cut out, what will be welded in, whether nearby rust is likely, and whether the repair will be protected afterwards.

Frequently asked questions

Can surface rust fail an MOT?

Usually not by itself, unless it affects structure, safety or creates dangerous sharp edges.

Can rusty sills fail MOT?

Yes, especially if the sill is holed, weakened or close to safety-related mounting points.

Can rust be repaired for MOT?

Yes, but repairs must restore strength properly. Covering holes with filler is not enough.

Is welding always worth it?

Not always. It depends on vehicle value, rust extent and overall condition.

Can I drive after failing on rust?

Not if the defect is dangerous. For major defects, only drive if legal and roadworthy.

Can underseal hide MOT rust?

Yes. Underseal can hide corrosion, but it will not make weak or holed metal safe.

Can brake pipe corrosion fail MOT?

Yes. Brake pipes that are badly corroded, weakened or leaking can fail the MOT.

Best next step?

Get the rust location inspected properly and compare repair cost with the car’s value.
