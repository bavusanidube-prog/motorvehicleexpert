Car Cranks But Won’t Start

If the engine turns over but the car will not start, the starter motor is usually doing its job. The fault is more likely to involve fuel delivery, ignition spark, immobiliser approval, weak voltage while cranking, engine sensors or engine management.

Quick answer

If a car cranks but will not start, the engine is turning over but not firing. Common causes include no fuel, no spark, immobiliser problems, crankshaft sensor faults, weak battery voltage under load or engine management issues.

This is different from a car that clicks but does not crank. If your car only clicks, read our starter motor clicking guide. If the car will not start but the battery seems fine, see car won’t start but battery seems fine.

Cranking vs clicking: why it matters

Cranks but will not start

The starter turns the engine, but the engine does not fire. This usually points towards fuel, ignition, immobiliser, sensor or ECU control faults.

Clicks but does not crank

The engine does not turn over. This usually points towards the battery, terminals, earth strap, starter motor, starter relay or starting circuit.

What the starting sound can tell you

Strong normal cranking

The starter is turning the engine properly, so fuel, spark, immobiliser and sensor checks become more important.

Slow cranking

The battery may be weak under load, even if the lights and dashboard still work.

Fast uneven cranking

Can sometimes point to low compression or timing problems, especially if the sound is unusual.

Starts then cuts out

May suggest immobiliser, fuel pressure, sensor or engine management issues.

Splutters but will not run

Often suggests fuel delivery, ignition misfire, flooding or poor sensor readings.

Only clicks

That is not a true crank-no-start fault. Check battery, terminals, earth strap and starter motor first.

Common causes when a car cranks but won’t start

No fuel reaching engine

Fuel pump, relay, fuse, filter or pressure faults can stop the engine firing.

No ignition spark

Coils, spark plugs, wiring or ignition control faults can prevent combustion.

Immobiliser issue

The key may be recognised enough to crank but still prevent starting on some vehicles.

Crankshaft sensor fault

The ECU may not know engine position, so fuel and spark timing may fail.

Camshaft sensor fault

Can cause long cranking, poor starting or complete no-start conditions.

Weak battery voltage

The engine may crank, but voltage may drop too low for electronics to work properly.

Flooded petrol engine

Too much fuel can stop a petrol engine starting cleanly.

Timing issue

A slipped belt or chain fault can cause serious no-start problems.

Engine management fault

Stored fault codes can help identify sensor, fuel or ignition problems.

What to check first

  • 1Confirm the engine is cranking strongly, not just clicking.
  • 2Check whether the immobiliser or key warning light stays on.
  • 3Listen for a fuel pump prime when the ignition turns on.
  • 4Check whether the engine almost starts then cuts out.
  • 5Look for engine management, battery or security warning lights.
  • 6Think about whether the fault happens hot, cold or after standing.
  • 7Avoid repeated cranking because it can flatten the battery.

Can the battery still be the problem?

Yes. A weak battery can sometimes crank the engine but still drop voltage too low for the ECU, fuel pump, injectors or ignition system to work correctly. This is especially possible in cold weather or if the battery is old.

If the engine cranks slowly, the dashboard dims heavily, or the car has recently needed jump starts, test the battery properly. You may also find these guides useful: how to check car battery health and car battery keeps going flat.

Does it happen hot, cold or after standing?

Won’t start cold

Battery weakness, glow plugs on diesel cars, fuel pressure or temperature sensor faults may be involved.

Won’t start hot

Crank sensor, fuel pressure or heat-related electrical faults are common possibilities.

After standing

Battery drain, fuel pressure loss or immobiliser/key issues may become more noticeable.

Diesel car cranks but won’t start

Diesel engines can have no-start faults linked to fuel pressure, glow plug systems, crank sensors, air in the fuel system, blocked filters or DPF-related problems.

If the car previously showed diesel warning lights, poor starting, limp mode or smoke, proper diagnostics are recommended before replacing parts. You may also want to read the DPF warning light guide if a diesel warning appeared before the no-start problem.

Petrol car cranks but won’t start

Petrol cars commonly need fuel, spark, compression and correct timing to start. If one of those is missing, the engine may turn over normally but refuse to fire.

Fuel issue

Fuel pump, relay, fuse, blocked filter or pressure loss can stop starting.

Spark issue

Coils, plugs, wiring or crank sensor problems can stop ignition.

Flooding

Repeated short starts or failed attempts can leave too much fuel in the cylinders.

Sensor fault

Crank, cam, coolant temperature or airflow readings can affect starting.

When to stop guessing

If the car repeatedly cranks but will not start, fault-code reading and proper checks are usually needed. A good diagnostic process may check battery voltage under load, fuel pressure, spark, injector pulse, immobiliser status and crank/cam sensor signals.

Guessing can quickly become expensive because many no-start faults feel similar from the driver’s seat. Replacing the fuel pump, battery, starter or sensors without testing can waste money.

Frequently asked questions

Why does my car crank but not start?

The engine may be missing fuel, spark, correct sensor signals, compression or immobiliser approval.

Can the battery still be the problem?

Yes. The engine may crank but voltage can still drop too low for reliable starting, especially with an old or weak battery.

Can a fuel pump cause crank no start?

Yes. If fuel pressure is missing or too low, the engine may crank without firing.

Can an immobiliser allow cranking?

On some cars, yes. The engine may crank but fuel or spark may be disabled.

Should I keep cranking it?

No. Repeated attempts can flatten the battery, overheat starter components and make diagnosis harder.

Is this a starter motor problem?

Usually not if the engine cranks strongly. Starter faults are more likely when it clicks or does not turn over.
