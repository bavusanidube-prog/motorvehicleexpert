MOT guide

Most common MOT failure reasons in the UK

Many MOT failures are caused by simple, visible problems such as worn tyres, faulty lights, poor wipers, brake issues, suspension wear and dashboard warning lights. This guide explains what commonly fails and what to check before your test.

Quick answer: why do cars fail MOT tests?

Cars commonly fail MOT tests because of defects in lights, tyres, brakes, suspension, visibility, emissions or safety systems. Some failures are serious mechanical faults, but many are basic issues that could be spotted before the test.

A simple pre-MOT check can reduce the chance of avoidable failure, especially if you inspect bulbs, tyres, wipers, washer fluid, mirrors, number plates and warning lights before booking the test.

Most common MOT failure reasons

  • 1Lights and indicators: failed bulbs, cracked lenses, poor alignment or brake lights not working.
  • 2Tyres: low tread depth, sidewall cuts, bulges, uneven wear or incorrect tyre fitment.
  • 3Brakes: worn pads, damaged discs, poor brake performance, imbalance or brake fluid issues.
  • 4Suspension: worn bushes, broken springs, leaking shock absorbers or knocking components.
  • 5Visibility: damaged windscreen, poor wipers, empty washer bottle or blocked washer jets.
  • 6Emissions: smoke, exhaust leaks, catalytic converter issues, DPF faults or engine running problems.
  • 7Warning lights: ABS, airbag, engine management or other safety-related dashboard warnings.
  • 8Number plates and mirrors: damaged plates, unreadable registration, missing mirrors or poor visibility.

Tyres and wheels

Tyres can fail if tread is too low, sidewalls are damaged, cords are exposed or the tyre is unsuitable for the vehicle. Uneven wear may also suggest alignment or suspension problems.

Lights and electrics

Headlights, brake lights, indicators, fog lights and number plate lights should all work correctly. A single failed bulb can be enough to cause a fail.

Brakes and suspension

Brake imbalance, worn components, leaks, weak handbrake performance, broken springs or worn suspension joints can all lead to MOT failure.

Simple checks before your MOT test

1. Check all lights

Test headlights, sidelights, brake lights, indicators, hazard lights, fog lights and number plate lights.

2. Inspect tyres

Look for low tread, cracks, bulges, exposed cords, nails, uneven wear and incorrect tyre pressure.

3. Check visibility

Make sure wipers clear properly, washers spray correctly and the windscreen is not badly damaged.

4. Watch warning lights

Dashboard warning lights for safety, braking, airbag, ABS or emissions systems should be investigated before the test.

5. Listen for noises

Knocking, grinding, scraping or humming noises can point to suspension, brake or wheel bearing faults.

6. Check number plates

Registration plates should be secure, clean, readable and not cracked or badly damaged.

Do warning lights fail an MOT?

Some dashboard warning lights can cause MOT failure, especially when they relate to tested safety or emissions systems. Common examples include ABS, airbag, brake system, power steering and engine management warnings.

If a warning light stays on after the engine starts, do not ignore it. A diagnostic check can help identify whether the issue is a sensor fault, wiring problem, mechanical fault or emissions-related issue.

Why MOT prevention matters

A failed MOT can create extra cost, delay and inconvenience. If the previous MOT has expired, you may also be restricted in how and where the vehicle can be driven.

Treat the MOT as part of regular maintenance rather than a surprise yearly event. If your car already has braking, steering, suspension, visibility, tyre or warning light problems, get them checked before the test date.

Best pre-MOT approach

  • ✓Book repairs early instead of waiting until the MOT expires.
  • ✓Check simple items at home before the test.
  • ✓Do not ignore warning lights or unusual noises.
  • ✓Keep servicing up to date to reduce avoidable failures.
  • ✓Use MOT advisories as early warnings for future repairs.

Frequently asked questions

Can a bald tyre fail an MOT?

Yes. A tyre below the legal tread limit, badly damaged or unsafe can fail the MOT immediately.

Can faulty brake lights fail an MOT?

Yes. Brake lights are safety-related and must work correctly.

Will an engine management light fail an MOT?

It can, especially if the warning relates to emissions or the light remains illuminated during the test.

Should I service my car before an MOT?

It can help. A service may identify worn brakes, tyres, lights, fluids or other faults before the MOT.

Can I drive after an MOT failure?

It depends on the failure, the previous MOT status and whether the car is roadworthy. Dangerous defects should not be driven until repaired.
