Common MOT Failure Reasons in the UK

Many MOT failures are caused by simple visible problems such as worn tyres, faulty lights, poor wipers, brake wear, suspension faults, emissions issues and dashboard warning lights. This guide explains the main fail areas, what they mean and what to check before your test.

Quick answer

Cars commonly fail MOT tests because of defects in tyres, lights, brakes, suspension, steering, visibility, emissions, number plates, body condition or safety systems.

Many failures are avoidable with a simple pre-test inspection covering bulbs, tyres, wipers, washers, mirrors, warning lights, number plates and obvious noises.

Most common MOT failure reasons

  • 1Lights: failed bulbs, cracked lenses, poor aim, brake lights not working or number plate lights out.
  • 2Tyres: low tread, cuts, bulges, exposed cords, incorrect fitment or severe uneven wear.
  • 3Brakes: worn pads, poor efficiency, imbalance, seized parts, brake fluid leaks or parking brake problems.
  • 4Suspension: broken springs, worn bushes, leaking dampers, ball joints, lower arms or anti-roll bar links.
  • 5Visibility: damaged windscreen, poor wipers, faulty washers, cracked mirrors or blocked view.
  • 6Emissions: excessive smoke, engine faults, exhaust leaks, catalytic converter faults or DPF issues.
  • 7Warning lights: ABS, airbag, brake, TPMS, ESC, traction control or engine management warnings.
  • 8Rust and structure: corrosion near suspension, seatbelt, brake or structural mounting points.
  • 9Steering: excessive play, leaking power steering, worn rack, track rod ends or poor alignment symptoms.
  • 10Registration plates: unreadable, insecure, incorrectly spaced or not illuminated correctly.

Tyres and wheels

Tyres can fail for low tread, dangerous damage, bulges, exposed cords or structural defects. Wheel bearings and insecure wheels can also become MOT problems.

Simple checks before your MOT

1. Check all lights

Test headlights, sidelights, brake lights, indicators, fog lights, reverse lights and number plate lights.

2. Inspect tyres

Check tread depth, sidewalls, pressure, nails, bulges, cracks and uneven wear.

3. Test wipers and washers

Wipers should clear the screen properly and washers should spray enough fluid.

4. Watch warning lights

Investigate ABS, airbag, brake, TPMS, ESC or engine lights before the test.

5. Listen for noises

Knocking, grinding, humming or scraping can indicate brake, suspension, bearing or steering faults.

6. Check number plates

Plates should be secure, readable, correctly spaced and properly illuminated.

7. Look underneath

Check for obvious rust, oil leaks, exhaust damage, hanging parts or brake fluid leaks.

8. Read old advisories

Previous advisories often become failures if ignored for another year.

Do warning lights fail an MOT?

Yes, many dashboard warning lights can affect MOT results if they relate to tested safety, braking, emissions or stability systems. Do not assume a car will pass just because it drives normally.

  • !ABS warning light: can fail if the ABS system indicates a fault.
  • !Airbag or SRS warning: can fail because it relates to occupant safety.
  • !Brake warning light: should be checked urgently before driving or testing.
  • !Engine management light: may fail if emissions-related or active during the test.
  • !TPMS warning: may affect the result on vehicles where the system is required.
  • !ESC or traction control light: can indicate a safety-system fault.

Which MOT failures are usually cheap or expensive?

Not every MOT failure is a disaster. Some are simple fixes, while others can become expensive because of labour, parts access or safety-critical components.

Often lower cost

Bulbs, wipers, washer fluid, simple number plate issues, minor clips or small visible fixes.

Moderate cost

Tyres, brake pads, discs, springs, shock absorbers, track rod ends or wheel bearings.

Can be expensive

Rust welding, emissions faults, catalytic converters, DPF issues, steering racks or complex electrical faults.

Why MOT history matters when buying a used car

MOT history can reveal repeated failures, corrosion concerns, mileage issues and signs of poor maintenance. A car with a valid MOT can still have advisories that become expensive later.

  • ✓Check repeated advisories year after year.
  • ✓Look for corrosion trends around sills, suspension mounts and subframes.
  • ✓Compare MOT mileage with adverts, invoices and service history.
  • ✓See whether failures were repaired promptly or ignored.
  • ✓Be cautious if the same tyres, brakes, leaks or rust advisories keep returning.

How to avoid an MOT failure

Treat the MOT as part of regular maintenance rather than a surprise yearly test. Fix advisories early and investigate noises or warning lights before they become expensive failures.

  • ✓Book the MOT early enough to leave time for repairs.
  • ✓Do quick checks at home before the appointment.
  • ✓Keep servicing up to date.
  • ✓Use advisories as future warning signs.
  • ✓Do not ignore brake, tyre, suspension or steering symptoms.
  • ✓Repair rust early before it becomes structural.

What to do if your car fails its MOT

Read the failure sheet carefully and separate urgent safety defects from smaller items. Ask the test centre what needs repairing, whether a partial retest is available and how long you have to return.

Do not keep driving a car that is unsafe or has dangerous defects. For more detail, read MOT retest rules UK.

Frequently asked questions

What are the most common MOT failures?

Common MOT failures include lights, tyres, brakes, suspension, visibility, emissions, warning lights, rust, steering and number plate issues.

Can a bald tyre fail an MOT?

Yes. A tyre below the legal tread limit or in unsafe condition can fail immediately.

Can faulty brake lights fail an MOT?

Yes. Brake lights are safety-related and must work properly.

Will an engine management light fail an MOT?

It can, especially if it is emissions-related or illuminated during the test.

Should I service my car before an MOT?

It can help identify worn tyres, brakes, fluids, lights or faults before the test.

Can I drive after an MOT failure?

It depends on the defect, whether the previous MOT is still valid and whether the vehicle remains roadworthy. Dangerous defects should not be ignored.
