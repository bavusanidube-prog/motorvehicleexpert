MOT Guide

Common MOT Failure Reasons in the UK

Many MOT failures are caused by simple visible problems such as worn tyres, faulty lights, poor wipers, brake wear, suspension faults, emissions issues and dashboard warning lights. This guide explains the most common fail areas and what to check before your test.

Quick answer

Cars commonly fail MOT tests because of defects in tyres, lights, brakes, suspension, steering, visibility, emissions, number plates or safety systems.

Many failures are avoidable with a simple pre-test inspection covering bulbs, tyres, wipers, washers, mirrors, warning lights and obvious noises.

Most common MOT failure reasons

  • 1Lights: failed bulbs, cracked lenses, poor aim or brake lights not working.
  • 2Tyres: low tread, cuts, bulges, exposed cords or severe uneven wear.
  • 3Brakes: worn pads, poor efficiency, imbalance, seized parts or leaks.
  • 4Suspension: broken springs, worn bushes, ball joints or leaking dampers.
  • 5Visibility: damaged windscreen, poor wipers or faulty washers.
  • 6Emissions: smoke, engine faults, exhaust leaks, catalytic converter or DPF issues.
  • 7Warning lights: ABS, airbag, brake, TPMS or engine management lights.
  • 8Plates & mirrors: unreadable registration plates or damaged mirrors.

Tyres and wheels

Simple checks before your MOT

1. Check all lights

Test headlights, sidelights, brake lights, indicators, fog lights and number plate lights.

2. Inspect tyres

Check tread depth, sidewalls, pressure, nails, bulges and uneven wear.

3. Test wipers & washers

Ensure blades clear well and washer fluid sprays correctly.

4. Watch warning lights

Investigate ABS, airbag, brake, TPMS or engine lights before the test.

5. Listen for noises

Knocking, grinding, humming or scraping can indicate faults.

6. Check number plates

Plates should be secure, readable and correctly illuminated.

Do warning lights fail an MOT?

Yes, many dashboard warning lights can affect MOT results if they relate to tested safety or emissions systems.

  • !ABS warning lights can fail.
  • !Airbag or SRS warnings can fail.
  • !Brake system warnings should be checked urgently.
  • !Engine management and TPMS lights may also matter.

Why MOT history matters when buying a used car

MOT history can reveal repeated failures, corrosion concerns, mileage issues and signs of poor maintenance.

  • ✓Check repeated advisories year after year.
  • ✓Look for corrosion trends.
  • ✓Compare MOT mileage with adverts and service history.
  • ✓See whether failures were repaired promptly.

How to avoid an MOT failure

Treat the MOT as part of regular maintenance rather than a surprise yearly test. Fix advisories early and investigate noises or warning lights before they become expensive failures.

  • ✓Book repairs before the MOT expires.
  • ✓Do quick checks at home first.
  • ✓Keep servicing up to date.
  • ✓Use advisories as future warning signs.
  • ✓Do not ignore brake, tyre or steering issues.

Frequently asked questions

Can a bald tyre fail an MOT?

Yes. A tyre below the legal tread limit or unsafe condition can fail immediately.

Can faulty brake lights fail an MOT?

Yes. Brake lights are safety-related and must work properly.

Will an engine management light fail an MOT?

It can, especially if emissions-related or illuminated during the test.

Should I service my car before an MOT?

It can help identify worn tyres, brakes, fluids or faults before the test.

Can I drive after an MOT failure?

It depends on the defect, previous MOT status and whether the vehicle remains roadworthy.
